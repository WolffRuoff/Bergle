	<header class="entry-header">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/10/12/blog-post-4-gender-gap-in-gaming-and-other-misconceptions/" title="Permalink to Blog Post #4 &#8211; Gender Gap in Gaming, and other Misconceptions" rel="bookmark">Blog Post #4 &#8211; Gender Gap in Gaming, and other Misconceptions</a></h2>
	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p>Every person has an image of what a gamer looks like, and unfortunately, many opinions are based off of misconceptions. It may be difficult to understand a gamer from a non-gamer’s perspective, and Maeve Duggan of the Pew Research Center proves that many people are misinformed about gaming. In most of the infographics, those who answered “unsure” made up an unsettling portion of the population (around 20%); it made me question the validity of the results. Even those who identify as gamers may have stereotypes about gaming. According to the survey, “men and women are equally likely to assume that most video game players are men regardless of whether they themselves play games” (Duggan, 2015, p. 3). I have though about this stigma as it applies to my own life, because I used to (and sometimes still do) play games a lot. I have had a lot of boys respond “Oh, that’s cool” when I tell them I play games, but I feel like there is a different reaction if a male identifies as a gamer. He is generally perceived as lazy, as if all he does is play games. Yet, when a female plays games, she is seen as more masculine and that is “cool”? Although these are generalizations, I find these things true in my own life.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>However, I can only imagine that a the idea of a female gamer has become more common over the past decade. As it becomes more and more acceptable for a female to act masculine and vice versa, the stigma of gaming is changing. Just eight years ago, the ratio of males to females in terms of gamers was 13:7, when now it is practically equal (Lenhart, 2008, p.1). I think that this stereotype will begin to fade as time goes on. It will become “normal” for a girl to identify as gamer. Eliminating sexist stigmas like this will eventually lead to more equality for all people. The longer people hold on to ideas, the longer it will take us to progress as a society. So, next time a female tells you she plays games, do not think of it as abnormal, consider her like any other gamer.</p>

However through this activity i began to realize the differences in the type of information one would search for. Thousands upon thousands of articles came up about arbor day. However when I typed in a specific foundation on arbor day less information came up. In general information it is better to keep the searches general to obtain more articles. </span></p>

I also think that having this google identity will immediately change people’s perceptions or first impressions of you. For example I said before how if you googled me nothing would come up. <article id="post-3789" class="post-3789 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-uncategorized">

	<div class="entry-details">
				<p>October 11, 2016<br/>
		by Lauren Smartenko<br/>
		<a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/10/11/blog-post-4-peer-facilitation/#respond">0 comments</a></p>
	</div><!-- end entry-details -->
    
	<header class="entry-header">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/10/11/blog-post-4-peer-facilitation/" title="Permalink to Blog Post #4-Peer Facilitation" rel="bookmark">Blog Post #4-Peer Facilitation</a></h2>
	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p>Social media: it consumes us in every way. Think about this; when was the last time you picked up your phone to check Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, Snapchat, etc.? Five minutes ago? Just now? Today, social media is addictive and has a variety of effects on us and society.</p>
<p>In particular, Instagram is a social media account that greatly effects the way we look at society, and most importantly ourselves. In an article I found online called &#8220;Selfie Loathing&#8221; by Jessica Winter, the horrors of Instagram are exposed. It focuses on the negative effects looking through Instagram feeds can have on our  self esteem. Because it is completely image driven, it causes an &#8220;envy spiral&#8221; that is extremely unhealthy (Winters). Going off of this point, Instagram has turned into something where we tend to fish for compliments. For example, posting a selfie gives off the message that you are looking for positive reinforcement in your appearance. Social media should not be something you go to when you want to hear something nice about yourself for a quick confidence booster.</p>
<p>The most detrimental effect of Instagram is the impact it has on young girls. Today, the majority of young girls have Instagram accounts, and follow people like Kylie and Kendall Jenner and models. These people get their images photoshopped before they are posted on Instagram. Being a young girl, you may not make that connection, causing you to feel bad about the way you look compared to these photoshopped models. This can very negatively effect self confidence in young girls. Also, these &#8220;Instagram famous&#8221; people tend to set the standards for what is socially acceptable to post on Instagram. The more proactive posts they put up, the more provocative posts younger girls will put up. Younger kids will see these people as role models, and if they are not setting the right standard, these young girls will think posting things that may get them in trouble is okay.</p>
<p>There are many complications when it comes to Instagram, and all social media. We need to use it with care and think about everything we post. We cannot pay attention to &#8220;fake&#8221; people online, and must not let it get to us emotionally. As a society, we have the power to work together and change the way society looks at Instagram, but we have to do it together.</p>
<p>Winter, J., &amp; Martinelli, M. (2013, July 23). Selfie-Loathing. <i>Slate</i>. Retrieved from http://www.slate.com/articles/technology/technology/2013/07/instagram_and_self_esteem_why_the_photo_sharing_network_is_even_more_depressing.html</p>

	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p><span lang="EN-US">In today’s internet community, not only famous people, but also normal people can be seen by the public through search engines or some social media. And the thing we should do first in this open community is to try to know what other people see through my social media accounts. So I use an afternoon to pretend I am a stranger and searching information through Google to try to know a person named JiahuiHuang. First of all, I entered the name JiahuiHuang in the Google engine to see if something shows. But the result really disappointed me, no matter if I click on ALL, Images, or Videos, the only place I can find myself is the LinkedIn—I am the eighteenth in line. To have a more accurate result, I also tried to search my information from other search engines like Bing and DuckDuckGo, though the messages they showed are different, I can’t find more information about myself. I started to think if my mother tongue is not English, and I stayed in a country that speak Chinese in my past eighteen years that would influence the result. So I searched my information in Google again, but this time in Chinese. Again, I found nothing, because so many people in China are named JiahuiHuang and I am not famous. Then I added some key words during the searching like my primary school. Excellent, I finally found a few things about myself during this searching—some writings that were awarded and some other award certificates at my primary school, and no more can be found.</span></p>
<p><span lang="EN-US">Why do I have so little information in the internet? Few reasons</span><span lang="EN-US">: </span></p>
<p><span lang="EN-US">1. I am not famous</span></p>
<p><span lang="EN-US">2. I am not a person who is outstanding—famous in a particular field, won some special award or had some special achievements.</span></p>
<p><span lang="EN-US">3. I am kind of a person that out of the online social community, I post nothing and sometimes I use a pseudonym in some social media such as Facebook and Instagram.</span></p>
<p><span lang="EN-US">Is it a great thing that others will have a hard time to find my real information through the network? From a security and privacy perspective, yes! But in my heart, I will feel uncomfortable that others can’t find something about me, because this proves I am not an outstanding person. In the future, though I will still not post something about myself, I hope others can find more about me through searching in Google. And what I hope to be seen in the future network in five years is something like this: JiahuiHuang, an outstanding student who used to study in Muhlenberg; Stanford student, JiahuiHuang, assists the professor in the important study of a subject; JiahuiHuang, reports an essay in a scientific magazine… </span></p>

<article id="post-3781" class="post-3781 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-blog-assignment tag-socialmedia tag-fake tag-followers tag-instagram tag-likes">

	<div class="entry-details">
				<p>October 11, 2016<br/>
		by Sarah Kremen<br/>
		<a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/10/11/defining-yourself-by-numbers/#respond">0 comments</a></p>
	</div><!-- end entry-details -->
    
	<header class="entry-header">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/10/11/defining-yourself-by-numbers/" title="Permalink to Defining Yourself by Numbers" rel="bookmark">Defining Yourself by Numbers</a></h2>
	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p>I&#8217;d be lying if I said I don&#8217;t care about how many likes my Instagram posts get. If I don&#8217;t get as many likes as I expected for a post, a part of me feels disappointed. There&#8217;s something about getting a lot of likes that is so fulfilling and satisfying. When I first got an Instagram, I was always jealous of people who got more likes than me. I was so desperate at one point, that I used #likesforlikes to get strangers to like my posts. Why must we be in this preconceived notion that we are defined by numbers?</p>
<p>&#8220;I&#8217;m not against social sharing, I&#8217;m against the current status of social media.&#8221; -Essena O&#8217;Neill, a 19-year-old from Australia (BuzzFeedBlue 2015). From a glimpse at her Instagram, O&#8217;Neill looks like she has it all. Not only is she gorgeous and has a small figure, but she has over half a million followers on Instagram and gets thousands of likes. Who wouldn&#8217;t want to be like that? Well it turns out that her &#8220;perfect&#8221; life isn&#8217;t as perfect as it seems. Because she got so Instagram-famous, O&#8217;Neill felt this pressure to continue posting picture-perfect posts of herself. She became miserable because &#8220;she became addicted to the approval of her appearance&#8221;, and the only thing that made her feel better was getting more likes and followers (BuzzFeedBlue 2015). As a result, O&#8217;Neill changed the captions of her pictures to expose how these &#8220;candid&#8221; pictures were staged so that she would look her absolute best.</p>
<p>I feel that even though we live in an era of technological advancements, we should all take a step back and look at what is truly important. Family, friends, health, equality&#8230;values in life that can&#8217;t be defined by numbers. It is up to us social media users to get out of this habit of caring about likes and start caring about things that are authentic.</p>
<p>BuzzFeedBlue. (2015, November 4). Instagram Star Reveals The Truth Behind Her Photos. Retrieved from <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zsxSMdFC20E">https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zsxSMdFC20E</a></p>

	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p>Google is a resourceful search engine that will give people results on just about anything they type into the search bar.  Everybody who has access to technology uses google for schoolwork, homework, and to just look up information in this day and age.  Google is an incredibly valuable search engine because of how popular, helpful, and efficient it is.</p>
<p>After googling myself, I witnessed a lot of new information about me that I have never previously seen before.  First off, I googled my real name, Richard Venuto, and there was no information found about me whatsoever.  Although Richard is my real name, I go by Ricky as a nickname, and as a result, a lot of information formulated after I typed &#8220;Ricky Venuto&#8221; into the search bar.  I googled myself multiple times in the past because I was curious about the information that would appear in front of me.  Up to my junior year of high school, there was not a lot of information about me on google.  However, after my successful senior year campaign with football, a lot of football-related articles were present on google after googling my name.  When I googled my name yesterday, even more information about myself popped up.  I scrolled through the &#8220;All&#8221; tab on google, and the first link that appeared on google was my highlight tape that I created for my senior year of high school.  Some more links that appeared were my Muhlenberg College football profile, my Spring-Ford football profile, my twitter profile, my Facebook profile, and all of the articles written about me or articles that mentioned my name along with my statistics from my 2015 high school football senior season.  This was the first time that I really went in depth with googling myself, and as a result, I saw some articles written about myself and about my high school team, along with the articles mentioning my statistics that I have never seen before.  For example, I saw a link to a website mentioning my Quarterback passing ranks in the State of Pennsylvania, and saw that I had the fifth best completion percentage in the state, and the first best touchdown to interception ratio in the state.  That was very cool for me to witness because I never saw that information before.  Additionally, I looked at the images after googling my name, and I saw a lot of repetition; football pictures from me playing in games.  However, the first picture that came up when I googled my name was a picture of me reading a book to Elementary school students.  I am very fond of that picture because it shows how our high school team did stuff for the community.  In addition, there are pictures of my family and friends that come up on images.  Googling myself and looking deeply into it was an awesome experience because I saw new information about me that I have previously never seen before; this assignment had a very positive impact on me.</p>

	<header class="entry-header">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/10/11/google-myself-2/" title="Permalink to Google Myself" rel="bookmark">Google Myself</a></h2>
	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p>After searching myself on multiple different search engines, I found very interesting things. When ever I would search myself, the internet would for some reason try to auto correct my name from two l&#8217;s in Hillburn to one l. With my name spelled with one l it turns out the results would come up with a much wider variety of people compared to Hillburns with two l&#8217;s. At the top of all the pages were mainly football related that includes things like my hudl account, videos of football camps I attended and my highlights. What also came up was my twitter profile and tweets I have posted. This really enforces the fact that everything posted online can be seen and makes me think about how careful I have to be with what I post online. Just a few scrolls down comes up a man with a profile about him going to jail and that is something I definitely would not want people to get confused with for me. What also surprised me is that a few years back I searched myself and found that a division one lacrosse player with the same name as me would come up and now I do not see him on the same page as me. This experience was very eye opening and just reinforces the fact that social media can be very dangerous if you do not be careful and can influence peoples opinions on you without even meeting you.</p>

	<header class="entry-header">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/10/11/google-2/" title="Permalink to Google" rel="bookmark">Google</a></h2>
	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Despite going on multiple different search engines including bing and google, as well as using a computer other than my own, I was not able to find much information on myself. After scrolling through the first few pages of google, I finally found an article  that was written by my town’s newspaper regarding a community service project  I was a part of in middle school. I was quite surprised by this as I assumed my social media would pop up. Although I am careful with my privacy settings, I assumed my name on the profiles would match when with my google search ;however, that was not the case. Abby’s profile on linkedIn kept popping up but my name, abbey, did not pop up. </span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Confused by the lack of information that popped up, I tried typing in my full name, Abigail, instead of Abbey. Although results were still not on the first page, I did find my full name on my school’s website where they posted the honor role.  Although I may not have seen much information on myself, I still don&#8217;t know what is out there. I still don&#8217;t know what other people see as their google searches will all bring up different things. The internet is unpredictable and you have to be extra careful.  </span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
