		<p><iframe width="654" height="368" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/jjcOQIL4l_w?feature=oembed" frameborder="0" allow="autoplay; encrypted-media" allowfullscreen></iframe></p>
<p>Dublin City’s Hugh Lane Gallery is dedicated to continuing the vision of Sir Hugh Lane himself, who held the first public exhibition of Irish Art in 1904 in London and worked to keep the Gallery’s collection of modern art growing. His collection, which included the first public impressionist paintings collection, was later presented to Dublin City Council in 1908 and is on display here in Dublin today. The Council has continued to exponentially grow Lane’s collection, this year adding a special exhibition on Francis Bacon, including his studio and archive.</p>
<p>From a sociological perspective, art galleries are incredibly interesting; the way in which people interact with one another is very unique both to the particular gallery as well as galleries as a whole. There is a multitude of ways to appreciate art as well as how to behave in the gallery. I noticed many people sitting on benches across from the pieces taking it in for a long period of time. Others tended to mill about, squinting at the descriptions then taking a step back and looking at the piece for no more than 20 seconds. There were a handful of people with sketchpads recreating the various paintings and sculptures on a smaller scale. Those that were there with a companion or two commented on the pieces in hushed tones, rarely above a whisper. A father and daughter pair sat in front of one of the larger landscapes as the father explained Romanticism and how to identify different styles. The gallery is not huge, but it had an immense amount of open space and beautiful architecture. The light blue and white color scheme made it feel much larger. The ambient noise bounced off the walls and echoed through the building, yet it lent itself to the serene atmosphere.</p>
<p>The new exhibition focusing on Francis Bacon and his work keeps the ambient noise and relative silence from the main gallery, but puts it against darkly painted walls and an almost prison-like environment. Once the door to the exhibit slides open, all you can hear on top of the distant creaks and murmurs is the narration of a documentary about Francis Bacon and his work which plays in the first room of the collection. Stepping into this exhibit is like stepping into Bacon’s mind: jarring, confining, erratic, and paranoia-inducing. As compared to the main art gallery where you see patrons standing and contemplating, the Bacon exhibit seems to keep them hurrying along. Even the centerpiece of Bacon’s studio is not inviting, forcing the visitor to step into a small glass viewing box in order to see the whole picture. It cuts you off from the rest of the exhibit and really makes you feel what it’s like to be in that space. Another section of the exhibit features boxes with phones attached to them that delve into the history of each parcel, tool, or piece found in the studio. This, like the studio itself, seems to want to envelop the viewer and keep them invested, cutting them off from everything else and demanding their attention.</p>
<p>Source: http://www.hughlane.ie/about-us</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><b><i>Introduction</i></b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Domestication theory values the “broader contexts” of consumers’ lives, and how that relates to their own appropriation, objectification, incorporation, and conversion of technology (Silverstone &amp; Hirsch, 1992). This theory not only illustrates the ways in which people incorporate technology into everyday life, but also how individuals’ own values and priorities inform the use of smartphones (Haddon, 2018).  Smartphone use varies greatly, depending upon the user (Bertel 2017). Therefore, an individual’s phone can paint a picture about their “biographies” and values with regard to patterns of use (Haddon, 2018). </span></p>
<p><b><i>Interview with Brett Mathews Regarding Mobile Phone Usage</i></b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Brett’s use of his phone certainly highlights how prominent mobile communication is. Brett noted that he is “constantly” checking his phone in order to remain “in the loop” or to avoid certain unwanted social interaction. Although he refrains from using his phone while interacting with others in group situations, as he finds it to be an impediment to robust and meaningful conversation. This is reminiscent of Haddon’s (2018) mention of social constraints with regard to mobile objectification</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> or how the device is displayed or used. Not only is simultaneous mobile use and in-person communication difficult, but can be seen as rude. Brett’s mention of avoiding unwanted interaction also illustrates Bertel’s (2017)assertion of the individualized dimension of smartphone use; although Brett prioritizes communication, he also values media consumption as a means to separate himself from his actual environment. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Brett focuses on the importance of phones for communicative purposes; to me, phones are first and foremost about communication.” This is quite evident in his most predominantly used applications: Facebook messenger, Groupme, and Snapchat. Mobile communication is more than just having a means of planning when to meet up in person, but rather the interaction itself. This can provide the same sense of security one feels when they are with a group of individuals, while still being physically alone. This is also illustrated in Brett’s use of home screen pictures; his home screen is a picture of him and his friends together at a breast cancer walk. He likes to not only feel connected to his friends through communication but in physical reminders as well. His lock screen also holds meaning; it is a picture of a path he saw on his trip to Glendolagh. He chose this picture both for aesthetic reasons as well as a reminder to keep moving forward. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Of course, communication is not the only function of today’s smartphones. They are entertainment centers, music archives, and navigation systems. The lattermost function was especially important to Brett when he got lost in the woods. Freshman year, Brett attended a nighttime hike to see the Blood Moon on a hiking trail. His group was unfortunately separated from the others and subsequently, they got lost in the middle of the woods at one in the morning. His friends had lost cell service, Brett had not; he was able to load GoogleMaps. Although the app could not properly navigate him home as they were not on a main road, but he was able to use the map image to get himself and his friends to safety. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">With regard to organization of applications, Brett’s phone is very logically arranged. Those that are used most frequently are on the bottom row. Those that are used a lot, but not constantly, are on the first home screen. These include social media and mobile banking apps. “The less important [the app] is to me, the more swipes it takes to get there.”  This is a more utilitarian means of organization, versus those who arrange by color or simply the type of app. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Ultimately, Brett values communication over the other functions of his phone; although his phone is useful for dangerous situations and entertainment, he highly values feeling connected to his loved ones. Brett’s mobile use is indicative of his core values and beliefs with regard to loved ones. Brett constantly checks his phone in order to not only stay “in the loop” with regard to logistics of meeting up with friends, but also to feel connected to his loved ones. His use of his phone illustrates just how important his relationships are to him. He mentions media solitary consumption, though seems to primarily consume media that is produced by his friends and acquaintances, further exemplifying how much he values relationships and the communicative properties of his phone.</span></p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong><em>Interview with Colleen Guernier Regarding Mobile Phone Usage</em></strong></p>
<p>Domestication theory is an analysis of how we engage with technology, specifically how it works within the context of our everyday lives. It helps us “to appreciate how they acquire information and communication technologies, the nature of that access, their uses of the technologies, the location and timing of that use and how and why they talk about or otherwise display their devices and services. [We begin to understand their] circumstances, biographies… relations and negotiations with others.” (Haddon, 2018)</p>
<p>Colleen’s phone is a necessity in her everyday life; it serves as her alarm clock in the morning, a survival tool, her way to get around, her sole source of communication with her family, her news source, and even her means of exercise. It truly exists to her as a “Swiss army knife” (Ling and Donner, 2013, p13) with a multitude of vital functions that Colleen makes sure to employ as often as possible. Because of its versatility, Colleen’s phone has become integrated into her life, especially while abroad.</p>
<p>While in a foreign environment, Colleen relies on her phone to guide her whenever she becomes lost, but even more importantly to her, her phone allows her to stay in close contact with her family and friends back home. She is not used to being away from them for extended periods of time, so the immediate gratification of that communication gives her great comfort. The sense of comfort that her phone provides, though, doesn’t end there. Colleen recalled a story in which her phone served as a tool to protect herself. She noticed a few men following her and talking at her while she was walking alone and decided to use her phone to pin her location and send it to friends in case something happened, put her headphones in, and call her brother who was able to talk to her until she reached her destination. Her phone acted not only as a way for people to know her location, but also as a way for her to mentally and physically escape the situation, simultaneously warding off the men.</p>
<p>Colleen makes sure to dispense with apps she no longer uses, but keeps a consistent, familiar format to keep up with the muscle memory that has developed due to app placement. The apps always at the forefront are email, texts, chrome, and phone, which shows that Colleen’s main priorities on her phone are equal parts communication and utility. She tends to use snapchat more than any other social media, even further proving that she strongly values communication and staying connected with her friends, just to chat or to plan the occasional meetup.</p>
<p>Though Colleen uses her phone for a variety of purposes, it is clear that her primary thought about her phone is that it is vital for communication and a feeling of familiarity. She uses Snapchat, the most personal of social media apps due to its one-on-one nature, and has her phone and texting apps in her dock. Domestication theory makes it clear that Colleen’s phone usage has shaped how she deals with certain situations and how she generally goes about her day, employing her phone to make her day more manageable and her time away from friends more bearable.</p>
