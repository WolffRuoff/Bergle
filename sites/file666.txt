<p style="text-align: center"><span style="color: #800000"><b>Virtual Volumes?:</b></span></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><span style="color: #800000"><b>Investigating Students’ Interactions with Rare Books</b></span></p>
<p style="text-align: center">Thursday, September 14, 2017</p>
<p style="text-align: center">4:30 p.m. to 5:30 p.m.</p>
<p style="text-align: center">Trexler Library, Level A Concourse</p>
<p>Trexler Library welcomes 2017 Alumni Achievement Award winner Dr. Diane H. Sonnenwald &#8217;76 to speak about her research into human-rare book interaction and its implications for virtual and augmented reality technologies.</p>
<p><img data-attachment-id="2185" data-permalink="http://trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/events/sonnenwald/attachment/0909_sonnenwald_d_0003b/#main" data-orig-file="https://i1.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2017/08/0909_Sonnenwald_D_0003b.jpg?fit=2480%2C1748" data-orig-size="2480,1748" data-comments-opened="0" data-image-meta="{&quot;aperture&quot;:&quot;2.8&quot;,&quot;credit&quot;:&quot;Vincent Hoban&quot;,&quot;camera&quot;:&quot;NIKON D2Xs&quot;,&quot;caption&quot;:&quot;Professor Diane Sonnenwald, Head of School, UCD School of Information &amp; <p>Rare historic books and manuscripts provide unique insights into historic events, as well as everyday life, art, literature and culture across time and geographic locations. It is important to allow individuals access to rare books so they can experience their craftsmanship and learn from them, expanding their appreciation and understanding of the past, and with this knowledge expand their participation in contemporary society. This talk will present research investigating human-rare historic book interaction in order to develop technology requirements for virtual reality renderings of rare books that enable integrated haptic, audio, olfactory, visual and cognitive human-rare book interaction.</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Diane H. Sonnenwald is Emerita Professor and Chair, School of Information and Communication Studies, University College Dublin, Ireland. She has been a </span><span style="font-weight: 400">Distinguished Visiting Professor and Endowed Chair at the Graduate Institute of Library and Information Studies, National Taiwan Normal University, and an Adjunct Professor in Computer Science at </span><span style="font-weight: 400">the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. Before joining academia, she worked at Bell Communications Research and Bell Laboratories. She has a Ph.D. from Rutgers University.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">In 2011-2012 Diane served as President of the Association of Information Science and Technology (ASIS&amp;T). She was recently awarded a 2017 Muhlenberg College Alumni Achievement Award and the 2016 ASIS&amp;T Watson Davis Award. Other awards include the 2014 Elfreda A. Chatman Research Award, the 2013 ASIS&amp;T Lecture Series Award, a Fulbright Professorship, U.S. Army Research Laboratory Scientific Contribution Award, University of North Carolina Junior Faculty Research Award, ALISE Research Methodology Best Paper Award, and Bell Communications Research Award of Excellence.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Diane’s research focuses on human information behaviour in a variety of contexts, interdisciplinary collaboration, and evaluation of emerging and future technology. Diane has authored over 100 peer-reviewed publications. Her latest edited book, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400">Theory Development in the Information Sciences</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400">, is published by the University of Texas Press. </span></p>
<p>This lecture is open to members of the Muhlenberg Community and the public. Refreshments will be served. The event is co-sponsored by Muhlenberg College Alumni Affairs. For more information contact <a href="susanfalciani@muhlenberg.edu">Susan Falciani</a> / 484.664.3694.</p>

But the capital isn&#8217;t Washington, D.C., it&#8217;s New York City. George Washington hasn&#8217;t even been inaugurated yet; that&#8217;s coming in April, four months hence. And forget Bush vs. Clinton 2.0; Alexander Hamilton just received the greatest number of nominating votes to serve as a Congressional delegate, while those Muhlenberg brothers, Peter &#8220;General Pete&#8221; and Frederick Augustus, were elected from Pennsylvania.</p>

For every familiar component, such as this list of goods for sale, is something slightly odd&#8230;.</p>
<p>For example, why does the second line of this ad read &#8220;Juft arrived?&#8221; &#8220;A few Chefts&#8221;&#8230; What&#8217;s a &#8220;cheft?&#8221; Prior to 1800, two forms of the letter &#8220;S&#8221; were used; one we still use today, but the other, the <a href="http://typefoundry.blogspot.com/2008/01/long-s.html">&#8220;long S,&#8221;</a> fell into disuse.  Stretched so long that it resembled an &#8220;f,&#8221; was used at the beginning or in the middle of words, with our familiar &#8220;S&#8221; appearing at the end. This seems to have caused confusion 200 years ago also, because type manufacturers began phasing it out between 1795 and 1805.</p>
<p>And why are the prices in unrecognizable characters? How much is &#8220;12fp?&#8221; The <a title="USMint" href="http://www.usmint.gov/education/historianscorner/?action=history">United States Mint</a> and the Coinage Act of 1792, which established the dollar as the currency of the new states, had not yet come into being. The monetary system still in place was that of England.</p>
<p>And for anyone interested in going into the import/export business: maybe you&#8217;d like to buy a ship? Complete with a load of orange peel?</p>

<p>We can get an idea of what household object were valued by looking at this detailed list of stolen items. The was the quickest form of social media of its day: merchants and tradesmen were reading the <em>Packet </em>daily as a vital component of their business. Any of these items brought into their shops by someone looking to make a quick sale would raise a red flag.</p>
<p><a href="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Robbery.jpg"><img data-attachment-id="1885" data-permalink="http://trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/uncategorized/special-collections-archives-highlights-january-newsflash-1789-style/attachment/robbery/#main" data-orig-file="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Robbery.jpg?fit=803%2C1281" data-orig-size="803,1281" data-comments-opened="0" data-image-meta="{&quot;aperture&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;credit&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;camera&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;caption&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;created_timestamp&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;copyright&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;focal_length&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;iso&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;shutter_speed&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;title&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;orientation&quot;:&quot;1&quot;}" data-image-title="Robbery" data-image-description="" data-medium-file="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Robbery.jpg?fit=188%2C300" data-large-file="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Robbery.jpg?fit=642%2C1024" loading="lazy" class="alignleft  wp-image-1885" src="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Robbery.jpg?resize=396%2C632" alt="Robbery" width="396" height="632" data-recalc-dims="1" /></a>Not all of the headlines and ads make for easy reading, however. Amongst a New Year&#8217;s poem, an excerpt from a novel about the Far East, and an advertisement for &#8220;good leather fire buckets for Fire-Companies or individuals&#8221; are printed two ads offering slaves for sale. In 1780, Pennsylvania had passed  <a href="http://avalon.law.yale.edu/18th_century/pennst01.asp"><em>An Act for the Gradual Abolition of Slavery<span style="text-decoration: underline">,</span></em></a> the first such state law in the nation. &#8220;Gradual&#8221; meant that the children born to slaves would now be free, but the parents would remain in slavery. Slave numbers dwindled over the next 50 years, but the practice was not fully abolished until 1847.</p>
<p>Our historical newspaper collection spans the late-18th, the 19th, and the early 20th centuries, and includes titles from the <em>Northampton Farmer</em> to the <em>New-York Herald</em>. Please contact <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/collections//special-collections/">Special Collections</a> for further information or to set up an appointment.</p>

<p>The notably reclusive poet was an active correspondent,  and one of her particular friends during her later years was Eugenia Hall, the &#8220;Genie&#8221; to whom our letter is address. Though undated, the four-page letter was probably composed ca. 1885.  It appeared in the first published collection of Dickinson&#8217;s letters in 1894 (<a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/search~S0?/Xemily+dickinson+letters&amp;SORT=D/Xemily+dickinson+letters&amp;SORT=D&amp;SUBKEY=emily+dickinson+letters/1%2C21%2C21%2CB/frameset&amp;FF=Xemily+dickinson+letters&amp;SORT=D&amp;6%2C6%2C" target="_blank">811.4 D553L</a>, p. 427).</p>
<p><em>Dear &#8220;Genie,&#8221;</em></p>
<p><em>The love-flower you sent me is like a little vase of spice and fills the hall with </em>c<em>innamon.</em></p>
<p><em>You must have skillful hands to make such sweet carnations. Perhaps your doll taught you. I know that dolls are sometimes wise. Robins are my dolls.</em></p>

<p><em>I am glad you love the blossoms so well. I hope you love birds too. It is economical. It saves going to heaven.</em></p>
<p><em>Lovingly,</em><br />
<em> Coz. Emily.</em></p>
<p>This autograph letter, as well as documents signed by Abraham Lincoln, Johann Wolfgang von Goethe, and others, may be viewed upon request by visiting Special Collections.</p>
