<h3>September 2</h3>
<p><strong>Guest Artist: Nicholas Leichter</strong></p>
<p><a class="grouped_elements" title="nicholas-leichter" href="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/nicholas-leichter.jpg" class="grouped_elements" rel="tc-fancybox-group1419" rel="tc-fancybox-group1421"><img class="alignnone wp-image-967" src="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/nicholas-leichter.jpg" alt="nicholas-leichter" width="164" height="248" /></a></p>
<p><strong>Required: DTP II, Jazz III</strong></p>
<p><strong>Bio</strong>: Nicholas Leichter, whose slippery, rhythmic style slides between street and classical, does mash-up more than subliminal. ~ The New York Times. Nicholas Leichter Dance creates cultural narratives where movement tells the story. The works are carnivalesque, wherein the jolly relativity of all things is proclaimed by alternative voices. Whether choreographing The Rite of Spring or reinventing the black Wizard of Oz, the works de-privilege the authoritative voice of hegemony through their mingling of high culture with the profane.</p>
<p>Nicholas Leichters technique and repertory class will familiarize dancers with his unique fusion of contemporary dance-one that blurs the line between traditional, experimental, cultural and urban dance forms and styles, creating a form that is both dizzying and thought provoking. A knowledge, or experience, in a wide range of dance styles, including traditional modern, contemporary, West African, urban, street jazz, house or hip-hop is encouraged but not mandatory, however an openness to the mashup and deconstruction of styles is important. Working with Mr. Leichter students will discover/rediscover a deeper understanding of how to incorporate a wide range of movement forms and create a level of hybrid that is individual and responsive to their community and environment</p>
<h3>September 16</h3>
<p><strong>Guest Artist: Wallie Wolfgruber</strong></p>
<p><a class="grouped_elements" title="wallie-wolfgruber" href="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/wallie-wolfgruber.jpg" class="grouped_elements" rel="tc-fancybox-group1419" rel="tc-fancybox-group1421"><img class="alignnone wp-image-968" src="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/wallie-wolfgruber.jpg" alt="wallie-wolfgruber" width="223" height="335" /></a></p>
<p><strong>Required: Ballet V, Modern IV and V</strong></p>
<p><strong>Bio</strong>: Wallie has had a distinguished teaching, performing and choreographic career with a wealth of experience in the studio, on stage and behind the scenes and a performing career spanned over 20 years. She was a principal dancer in the Lar Lubovitch Dance Company, also serving as Mr. Lubovitchs choreographic assistant. She has worked with other notable names in modern dance such as Rod Rogers, Donald Byrd/The Group, Ohad Naharin and Sung Soo Ahn.</p>
<p><b>Wallie Wolfgruber</b> has enjoyed a distinguished directing, teaching, choreographic, and performing career. Since joining the Conservatory of Dance in July 2010 and serving as director through June 2014, she has brought a wealth of experience in the studio, on stage, and behind the scenes to the program. Ms. Wolfgruber holds an MFA in dance from NYU Tisch School of the Arts. Her directing and teaching experience includes being undergraduate program director and associate professor in the dance department at SUNY Brockport; co-founder and director of SEAD in Austria; and a faculty member at Florida State University, Rutgers University, Manhattanville College, and the Mark Morris Dance Center. Her teaching expertise encompasses ballet, modern technique, composition, and improvisation. In 2007, she travelled to Africa, researched Maasai dances, and taught and performed in orphanages in remote, rural areas in Kenya. Ms. Wolfgrubers choreography has been presented internationally and in New York City at the Abrons Arts Center, La Mama E.T.C., the Tribeca Performing Arts Center, and with DanceNow at the Joyce Soho, Joes Pub at the Public Theater, the Duke on 42nd Street and Dance Theater Workshop. Her interests include collaborations with composers and video artists. Her video dance, <i>A Hands-On Affair</i>, was created in collaboration with Alvin Booth and selected for the Dance On Camera Festival in Los Angeles and New York City. She established her company <i>Wallie Wolfgruber &amp; Dancers</i> in 2008. Her ongoing project, <i>Sacred Things</i>, currently includes the dance works <i>Dream Support</i> and <i>Ingress</i>, a series of photographs and an earth installation. Ms. Wolfgruber was a principal dancer with the Lar Lubovitch Dance Company, toured internationally, and has also worked with a variety of other choreographers from established to experimental. Among these are Ohad Naharin, Donald Byrd, Rod Rogers, Keith Young, Sung Soo Ahn, and Jessica Iwanson.</p>
<h3>September 30</h3>
<p><strong>Guest Artist:Paul Hudson</strong></p>
<p><strong>Required: Both Comp classes</strong></p>
<p><strong>Bio:</strong>Paul Hudson has been designing lighting for theater, dance, opera, television, and architectural public art in and around New York for over a decade. Having finished the graduate program at NYU at the age of 22, he knew from a young age that he wanted to sculpt with the intangible medium of light to help his fellow collaborators bring their creations to life.  His work has been seen throughout the New York area, in venues both large and small: from the Irish Rep to Gibney Dance; from Carnegie Hall to wacky, underground, site-specific locations. Paul has the good fortune to work with the Juilliard School on regular occasion, having lit operas and plays for the institution, as well as collaborating on Beyond the Machine for the past 6 years  an annual showcase of music, drama, dance, and other performance utilizing technology in new and interesting ways.</p>
<div dir="ltr">Recently, Paul designed the small but politically charged premiere of Private Manning Goes to Washington that traveled to Edinburgh, and then returned stateside to launch the second national tour of Basetrack, Live, a piece of verbatim theater looking at the experience of Marines serving in Afghanistan and returning home after their tours of duty. Internationally, Pauls work has also been seen throughout South America, with several dance companies, as well as in Cyprus, Spain, and London. Over the next several months, he is bringing Private Manning to New York; collaborating with several artists to create Cracked Orlando, a new opera with fractals at Juilliard, which includes several dance passages that will be incorporated into the larger projection design in lieu of traditional operatic ballet sequences; and continuing to workshop Sorry, a full-length, narrative dance-and-poetry performance addressing culture, class, and race, and whether love can actually surmount the difficulties those things sometimes create. He counts himself lucky to be able to light such a broad spectrum of work, from the joyous the tragic, and the profound to the prosaic</div>

<h3>November 4</h3>
<p><strong>Guest Artist: Boomerang</strong></p>
<p><a class="grouped_elements" title="boomerang" href="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/boomerang.jpg" class="grouped_elements" rel="tc-fancybox-group1419" rel="tc-fancybox-group1421"><img class="alignnone wp-image-961" src="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/boomerang-300x200.jpg" alt="boomerang" width="344" height="229" /></a></p>
<p>http://www.boomerangdance.com/</p>
<p><strong>Required: DTP II, Modern IV and V</strong></p>
<p>Bio: A daringly physical, poetically nuanced dance and performance project created in 2012 by co-artistic directors Matty Davis and Kora Radella with founding member Adrian Galvin. Recognizing the body as an evolving repository for both physical and psychological life, BOOMERANG sifts through and siphons from the rich, eclectic histories that constitute the personhoods of the people with whom they work.</p>
<p>The physical research of these classes demands athleticism and verve, as well as attention to detail. There will be lively investigations that utilize core strength and release technique principles to create an enlivened presence. Full-throttle and roller-coaster motion via momentum-based floor work and inversions will be met with point-on-precision. Dancers will learn about and experience the nuances of being on the edge of control.</p>
<p>Boomerangdance.com</p>

<h3>December 2</h3>
<p><strong>Guest Artist: Eva Dean</strong></p>
<p><a class="grouped_elements" title="eva-dean" href="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/eva-dean.jpg" class="grouped_elements" rel="tc-fancybox-group1419" rel="tc-fancybox-group1421"><img class="alignnone wp-image-963" src="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/eva-dean-300x200.jpg" sizes="(max-width: 359px) 100vw, 359px" srcset="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/eva-dean-300x200.jpg 300w, http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/eva-dean.jpg 600w" alt="eva-dean" width="359" height="239" /></a></p>
<p>http://evadeandance.org/</p>
<p><strong>Required: Dance and Society, Modern III</strong></p>
<p><strong>Bio: </strong>Eva Dean founded Eva Dean Dance (EDD) in 1985. Throughout the years her choreography has received critical acclaim and accolades from audiences, presenters and students. Dean views herself as a movement experimentalist and she is recognized as a consummate professional who creates work that uses multiple elements to bring her choreographic vision to life. Choreographic intent that embraces intuition is the spring- board for Deans creative process, and her unique movement is honed and crafted while working with her dance company. The dancers artistry and improvisational skills are an integral part of the work. Boots, balls, recycled materials and architectural design are specifically selected to enhance Deans artistic vision. Dean works closely with guest artists such as musicians, puppet masters, lighting, and set and costume designers to create a visually rich body of work, which, as a result, is diverse in scope, giving its audiences enduring memories</p>
<p>Students will use guided imagery to access and construct abstract, movement-based stories surrounding their personal experience. Students will move to the pulse of the 21st century, and celebrate the joy of slowing down while moving fast, remaining in the present moment. The workshop will begin with authentic movement and breathing exercises to help students become aware of their perceptions of time and movement efficiency. Students will then be guided into improvisational movement in pairs, learning techniques to give and receive weight, both on the ground and in the air. The workshop will end with melding together personal authentic movement, and the sharing and giving of weight to create short improvisational duets. This workshop is for all skill levels</p>

<h1><em><strong>Spring 2017</strong></em></h1>
<p><a class="grouped_elements" title="mark gidnik" href="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/mark-gidnik.jpeg" class="grouped_elements" rel="tc-fancybox-group1419" rel="tc-fancybox-group13"><img class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-1424" src="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/mark-gidnik-200x300.jpeg" sizes="(max-width: 200px) 100vw, 200px" srcset="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/mark-gidnik-200x300.jpeg 200w, http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/mark-gidnik-768x1151.jpeg 768w, http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2015/01/mark-gidnik-684x1024.jpeg 684w" alt="mark gidnik" width="200" height="300" /></a></p>
<p><strong>Friday, January 27</strong></p>
<p><strong>2:30-4PM, DST</strong></p>
<p><strong>Mark Gindick</strong></p>
<p><strong>Required: Composition, Circus</strong></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Mark Gindicks Clown and Technology class is designed to inform students how to change judgements about clown work. You will learn how to live in your body in regards to how clowning supports a comedic body, performing spirit, and interaction with the audience. Through eccentric dance and improvisation you will awaken your inner clown character and discover how to write your own physical comedy routines and how clowning reflects on present social media and technology.</p>
<p>Mark Gindick was born and raised in New York City. He studied film and drama at SUNY Purchase, and is a graduate of Ringling Bros. and Barnum &amp; Baileys Clown College. He has been working as a physical comedian onstage and in the circus since his graduation from Clown College, and has shared the role of Grandma with Barry Lubin at the Big Apple Circus in Big Top Doo Wop (2001-2002) and Dreams Of A City (2002-2003). Mark has performed as a clown with The NY Goofs at Carnegie Hall and at The International Childrens Festival, with Ringling Bros. &amp; Barnum &amp; Bailey, and has been featured in the Big Apple Circus production of Play On! in 2008-2009. He has written, performed and appeared in theater, film and television, including Nora Ephrons Julie and Julia (2009), The Late Show with David Letterman, and Late Night with Conan OBrien. Marks one-man theatrical show Wing-Man is sailing toward residencies, shows and workshops, with new creative multi-media platforms and directions on the current horizon. After work shopping his award-winning one-man show Wing-Man in places like NYC, Austin Texas, Rochester NY, and Warsaw Poland, Mark will be building his show in residency at the New York City College of Technology, Entertainment Division also known as City Tech. Marks working with his brand new amazing creative team, merging old school clowning with new school technology. For more information visit: wingmantheshow.com</p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Class Description</p>
<p>A meditative, intuitive, improvisational movement practice involving a mover and a witness. It is a practice of listening, attunement; slowing and opening to a deeper sensing of body and psyche. With eyes closed, maintaining a focus on bodily sensation and the flow of consciousness, the mover attunes to the inner landscape of the body and allows herself to be moved by whatever impulses are arising in the moment. With eyes closed it is easier to focus inwardly. The mover, in the presence of the witness, discovers the consciousness and compassion of their own inner witness.</p>
<p>paulajosajones.org/cgi-local/welcome.cgi</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Paulas background is as a dancer, choreographer, writer, visual artist, educator and equestrian. She is dedicated to nourishing improvisational practice in work and play, and discovering how to be non-habitual in mind and body. My research is embodied 100% of the time. My performing, coaching and teaching are about helping you find focus and live your most embodied, improvisational and joyful life. She believes in deepening creativity and awareness playfully, through the body, and finding ways to expand knowing with all fifty trillion cells, not just the ones perched on our necks. That means having a 24/7 wide-awake, multi-sensory, bodily experience of ourselves and the world.</p>

<p>Class Description</p>
<p>This class will focus our awareness and trust in the relationship between ourself and another moving body. We will study the responsibility two individuals must take on when working together to create one cohesive experience. Through momentum and successive articulation, we will move into and out of shared weight, finding the partnership to be as maneuverable a surface as the floor.</p>
<p>A native Michigander, Bryan Strimpel graduated from Wayne State University in 2011 with a B.F.A. in dance. He is Co-Director for his own company, B.S. Movemend and he has danced with Nicholas Leichter Dance from 2009 to 2012 as well as the Brian Brooks Moving Company from 2011-2014. Most recently Bryan is dancing with David Dorfman Dance. He has performed internationally and across the U.S. in venues including: The Joyce Theater, Joyce SoHo, Brooklyn Academy of Music, Jacobs Pillow, Bates Dance Festival, Joes Pub, Hundred Grand SoHo and more. He teaches class in NYC regularly at Gibney Dance at 280 Broadway. Bryan is honored to have been selected as one of Dance Magazines Top 25 to Watch in 2013.</p>
