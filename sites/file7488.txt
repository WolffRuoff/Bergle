					<div class="entry-content">
				<p>I was finally home for Thanksgiving dinner&#8230;and I was ready to eat some turkey.</p>
<p>Every year we have Thanksgiving dinner at my house, so all our family comes and many of them spend a few days with my mom, dad, brother, and I. We sat around watching the football game and eating appetizers until dinner was ready at about 4:30pm. This year, thanks to my mothers Pinterest addiction, we decided to do something new for saying grace. Each of us wrote down on a post-it note what we are thankful for, then they were read out loud and we tried to guess who said it. I said that I was thankful for my friends, family, and my education. Then the eating commenced.</p>
<p>The food was great, especially dessert (pumpkin pie, chocolate pie, cranberry pie, cookies, brownies, etc). Needless to say, I was comfortably full and had to nearly be rolled up to bed later that night.</p>
<p>My family has a tradition to go shopping at 3am on Black Friday. This year we continued the tradition for the 4th year in a row. My two aunts, my cousin, and I woke up and drove to shop at two outlets near by. My mom skips out on this adventure&#8230;she thinks we are crazy for doing this, but I think not.</p>

			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2012/11/chakula-na-asante-food-and-thanks/" rel="bookmark">Chakula na asante (Food and Thanks)</a></h2>

			<div class="entry-meta">
				<span class="meta-prep meta-prep-author">Posted on</span> <a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2012/11/chakula-na-asante-food-and-thanks/" title="4:22 pm" rel="bookmark"><span class="entry-date">November 26, 2012</span></a> <span class="meta-sep">by</span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/author/edward/" title="View all posts by Edward">Edward</a></span>			</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

					<div class="entry-content">
				<p>Hey there all &#8211; happy belated turkey day. While everyone back in the states was probably filling up on their favorite amino acid, tryptophan, me and my colleagues at Kilimanjaro Bush Camp were hard at work. A few days ago, our classes officially ended, which means that we have moved into the final phase of our time here, Directed Research.</p>
<p>The thirty of us have been divided into three groups, each addressing very different (yet all so intertwined) issues throughout the area. My personal research has to do with water resource management and erosion.</p>
<p>Thanksgiving day was actually our first day of field work for our research, and it has been an awesome couple of days since. Most of us don&#8217;t have big rubber boots, so the days have been spent trying to gather as much data about the riverine environment without actually going into the river (easier said than done). I feel like a veritable Indiana Jones/whoever the main guy in Jurassic Park is. We spend our days waking early, driving out to the river and really whacking our way through thick brush, vines, and of course, acacia thorns.</p>
<p>I&#8217;m grateful that my study area is full of so much natural beauty and that the terrain is so demanding. It&#8217;s like getting to go on a hike through African woodland and do research at the same time.</p>
<p>All that being considered, the days definitely take a lot out of me. Starting at 6:45 in the morning, we start by filling 20-liter water jugs to bring into the field, we arrive at the river at 8:30 and then hike for about 10km along the river as we collect data. That takes a majority of the day, and we return to camp for a small rest before meeting for debrief with the head of our research group at 5pm. Between the end of that meeting and dinner we enter the data we&#8217;ve collected into spreadsheets and following dinner we read primary literature and write our research proposal until I fall into bed at some of the earliest bed times my body has ever known. This is exactly what I hoped directed research would be like, and I can&#8217;t wait to get out into the field tomorrow.</p>
<p>I hope everyone made it through Thanksgiving and Black Friday in one piece (though we love them, family can often be as taxing as school or work). We are actually celebrating our African Thanksgiving on the 28th, a day of rest when we get a breather from the mad rush of research. It was certainly odd to think about what was happening back home while we were in the field for Thanksgiving. We were gathering the various groups together from a long day of research as the Macy&#8217;s parade was going on, I was burned out at the end of a few pieces of literature when I called my family in the midst of preparing a thanksgiving feast, and I was probably far into dreamland by the time everyone sat down to eat.</p>
<p>We went around last night at dinner and said what we were thankful for, which was a nice reminder of what day it was. We were all very thankful for the chance to have this once in a lifetime experience, for the new African family we were celebrating with, and of course for our families back home.</p>
<p>Some things I am thankful for: Being part of the most attractive research group, getting an awesome cultural experience with all the rich cultures around me, being born in a country where, by virtue of my birth, I am afforded a comfortable life and of course&#8230;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
