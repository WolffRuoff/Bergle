							<h2 class="s-lib-box-title">Race-related Protests in the United States, Film Documentation
                                </h2>
							<div id="s-lg-box-collapse-24135865" >
								<div class="s-lib-box-content">
									<div class="">
                        <ul id="s-lg-link-list-57702243" class="s-lg-link-list s-lg-link-list-2"><li class="">
                        
			<div id="s-lg-content-55103993" class=""><span><a href="http://gateway.proquest.com.muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/openurl?url_ver=Z39.88-2004&res_dat=xri:bsc:&rft_dat=xri:bsc:video:vidwpa56_08" target="_blank"  onclick="return springSpace.springTrack.trackLink({link: this,_st_type_id: '2',_st_content_id: '55103993'});">Civil rights march, Birmingham, Alabama, 1963.</a> 
				</span><div id="s-lg-link-desc-55103993" class="s-lg-link-desc">"Black ministers kneel in prayer during the 1963 Alabama Freedom March while a police officer waits to arrest them. They get into the police wagons willingly, in an example of Martin Luther King's policy of nonviolent resistance. In the background we can hear gospel singing."</div>
			</div></li>
                        <li class="">
                        
			<div id="s-lg-content-55104081" class=""><span><a href="http://gateway.proquest.com.muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/openurl?url_ver=Z39.88-2004&res_dat=xri:bsc:&rft_dat=xri:bsc:video:vidwpa05_33" target="_blank"  onclick="return springSpace.springTrack.trackLink({link: this,_st_type_id: '2',_st_content_id: '55104081'});">Civil rights protestors, Birmingham, Alabama, 1963.</a> 
				</span><div id="s-lg-link-desc-55104081" class="s-lg-link-desc">"In this clip civil rights activists protest against police brutality in Birmingham, Alabama in 1963. During the 1960s, Birmingham was one of the most racially violent and segregated cities in the southern states of America. Its police chief, "Bull" Connor, was particularly ruthless, authorising the use of both fire hoses and dogs on peaceful protestors. The protestors sing and hold placards that read "shame on Birmingham.""</div>
			</div></li>
                        <li class="">
                        
			<div id="s-lg-content-55104106" class=""><span><a href="http://gateway.proquest.com.muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/openurl?url_ver=Z39.88-2004&res_dat=xri:bsc:&rft_dat=xri:bsc:video:vidwpa50_18" target="_blank"  onclick="return springSpace.springTrack.trackLink({link: this,_st_type_id: '2',_st_content_id: '55104106'});">Film footage shows the Newark race riots in New Jersey in July 1967.</a> 
				</span><div id="s-lg-link-desc-55104106" class="s-lg-link-desc">"This clip shows scenes of the 1967 Newark race riots in New Jersey, including smashed store fronts, which were common occurrences during urban riots. The police presence is backed up by the National Guard as they try and bring the disorder under control."</div>
			</div></li>
                        <li class="">
                        
			<div id="s-lg-content-55104117" class=""><span><a href="http://gateway.proquest.com.muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/openurl?url_ver=Z39.88-2004&res_dat=xri:bsc:&rft_dat=xri:bsc:video:vidwpa05_31" target="_blank"  onclick="return springSpace.springTrack.trackLink({link: this,_st_type_id: '2',_st_content_id: '55104117'});">Ku Klux Klan march, Washington, DC, 1925.</a> 
				</span><div id="s-lg-link-desc-55104117" class="s-lg-link-desc">"This silent clip dates from 1925, and was shot in Washington, DC at a Ku Klux Klan march. In the clip, male and female marchers wear the organizationüs uniform of white hood and robe, which are intended to symbolize racial purity. At the time of the march the organization had a membership of five million. The march was a symbol of the KKK's revival after the "Jim Crow" laws of the 1890s had rendered it superfluous by securing the subordinate position of African Americans in society. During its resurgence the KKK began to target Jewish and Catholic immigrants as well as blacks."</div>
			</div></li>
                        <li class="">
                        
			<div id="s-lg-content-55104123" class=""><span><a href="http://gateway.proquest.com.muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/openurl?url_ver=Z39.88-2004&res_dat=xri:bsc:&rft_dat=xri:bsc:video:vidwpa58_07" target="_blank"  onclick="return springSpace.springTrack.trackLink({link: this,_st_type_id: '2',_st_content_id: '55104123'});">March on Washington, DC, August 1963.</a> 
				</span><div id="s-lg-link-desc-55104123" class="s-lg-link-desc">"This newsreel footage shows the 1963 March on Washington for equal rights and equal employment for black Americans. The film opens with shots of Washington, including the Abraham Lincoln memorial; the Civil Rights Movement anthem, "We Shall Overcome," is the backing track. We see demonstrators arriving from all over the country on buses and trains. The voiceover estimates that 100,000 people, including 30,000 white people, attended the march. The marchers sing and hold placards calling for equality under the law. Leading the march are US labor organizer and civil rights activist Philip Randolph, Roy Wilkins, the president of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People, trade unionist Walter Reuther, and Martin Luther King, Jr."</div>
			</div></li>
                        <li class="">
                        
			<div id="s-lg-content-55104147" class=""><span><a href="http://gateway.proquest.com.muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/openurl?url_ver=Z39.88-2004&res_dat=xri:bsc:&rft_dat=xri:bsc:video:vidwpa06_06" target="_blank"  onclick="return springSpace.springTrack.trackLink({link: this,_st_type_id: '2',_st_content_id: '55104147'});">Race riots in Detroit and Harlem are shown in this 1967 news footage.</a> 
				</span><div id="s-lg-link-desc-55104147" class="s-lg-link-desc">"This 1967 newsreel reports on the race riots of the late 1960s in Detroit and Harlem. With the Black Power movement blacks became more militant in their search for freedom under the leadership of Malcolm X, who stated "We are not nonviolent with any one who is violent with us." The news reader reports that anarchy, terror and lawlessness swept through the streets of Detroit and then Harlem. The reel closes with a strong message from the federal government as President Johnson affirmed 'We will not tolerate lawlessness.'"</div>
			</div></li>
                        <li class="">
                        
			<div id="s-lg-content-55104165" class=""><span><a href="http://gateway.proquest.com.muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/openurl?url_ver=Z39.88-2004&res_dat=xri:bsc:&rft_dat=xri:bsc:video:vidwpa08_09" target="_blank"  onclick="return springSpace.springTrack.trackLink({link: this,_st_type_id: '2',_st_content_id: '55104165'});">Selma-Montgomery march, March 1965.</a> 
				</span><div id="s-lg-link-desc-55104165" class="s-lg-link-desc">"British news footage illustrates international interest in the 1965 civil rights march from Selma to Montgomery, Alabama, led by Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. The film, entitled "Marchers demand civil rights," opens with shots of crowds in the streets of Selma. Martin Luther King is speaking and the voice-over announces that Selma is certain to have a place in the history of freedom."</div>
			</div></li>
                        <li class="">
                        
			<div id="s-lg-content-55104174" class=""><span><a href="http://gateway.proquest.com.muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/openurl?url_ver=Z39.88-2004&res_dat=xri:bsc:&rft_dat=xri:bsc:video:vidwpa06_05" target="_blank"  onclick="return springSpace.springTrack.trackLink({link: this,_st_type_id: '2',_st_content_id: '55104174'});">The Watts riots in Los Angeles, which took place in August 1965.</a> 
				</span><div id="s-lg-link-desc-55104174" class="s-lg-link-desc">"Scenes of destruction and burning buildings are filmed during the Los Angeles Watts race riots of 1965. Fires from the riots covered 100 blocks, and the fire damage alone was estimated at 200 million dollars. The Watts riots lasted for six days and resulted in 34 deaths, thousands of injuries and 4,000 arrests. The riots began in the predominantly black Watts area of the city, when a black man was stopped and accused of drunk driving. The riots broke out after he and his passengers were arrested following an argument with the police officers."</div>
			</div></li>
                        <li class="">
                        
			<div id="s-lg-content-55104934" class=""><span><a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bSeCpFCS32M" target="_blank"  onclick="return springSpace.springTrack.trackLink({link: this,_st_type_id: '2',_st_content_id: '55104934'});">Rodney King riots in Lost Angeles, 1992</a>

Reporter Linda Breakstone is live on the scene at LAPD’s Parker Center headquarters as the agitated crowd grows in numbers."</div>
