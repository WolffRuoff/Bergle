                            <h1>Dance Technique and Performance 2</h1>
                                <div class="new-text"><p>Theresa Regan &amp; Maya Pandey 10/29/18<br />
Journal #3 DTP2</p>
<p>(Theresa) This unit brought out tons of emotion and heavy opinions in everyone in our class. I felt the readings, conversations, and activities were so worthwhile and greatly needed. Not often do we get to come together as dancers and discuss the injustices and cruel assumptions/stereotypes in our field of study. Why is it that being a dance major on campus warrants other students to have preconceived ideas on how we should look? When did being this type of athlete/artist come with a predetermined femininity and body type? As dancers and future educators, we have a lot to evolve in this field to make it a safe place for worshiping all different bodies that can move as one.<br />
The activity where we traced our bodies on a giant piece of paper really stands out to me as the most important day of the unit. Seeing what my body looked like on paper was surprising. I picked a pose that seemed neutral and provided me with rest and stability. I am all about being a member of the self-love club so when we inevitability filled our bodies up with words of self-love, appreciation, and care, my heart was made to feel better. No matter how many positive things you write about yourself, I truly felt the love in the room where we got to write positive messages on other peoples full-size body drawing. Making other people realize their worth and that they are enough, made my day. No matter how much I love myself, it hurts me when I realize people in my space think so critically about themselves. It makes me wonder in what types of environments they were raised and their personal histories. Unfortunately, something that links all us is our love for dance, and with that came somebody shaming growing up, that could have been very detrimental to someone&#8217;s upbringing.<br />
From my screening with Gayanne, I was surprised about some of the results. How can someone understand my body more than I do just by looking at me once? She told me things I have never known before like how I have more natural turn in than turn out and how for a dancer I have a strong butt. I didn&#8217;t take any of her comments too seriously, I simply agreed I had to work on my core strength and I was on my way. But as I began dancing post visit I couldn&#8217;t help but second-guess the way in which my own body was moving across the dance floor. Why was I questioning something I have known my whole life? I realized that this information was all based on perspective. It&#8217;s not good or bad how my body is made up/functions because it is uniquely mine. Moving forward from this unit and dance screening I will now focus on things that help improve my body awareness and positivity as well as inspire others to understand their unique complex body greatness.<br />
(Maya) Because of this unit, the view on my body has not changed much when it comes to how it physically looks since I am not svelte like most dancers. But I have realized that there are actually things about my body that are positive and amazing and that I do not need to have the “Ideal” dancer’s body. With the readings, discussions, body screening, video and master classes I have realized that there actually some things about my body that are positive.<br />
One example was when we discussed articles in class and talked about our bodies and what it was like growing up for us. I remember talking about how one of the studios I grew in had teachers that would look at me and point out dancers that were toned and had abs and how we are all supposed to look like that because they are strong. I then said in our class how “we don’t have to look strong, to be strong”, and I am proof because I do not have abs or the most amazing body, but I can do the same thing as the people who do. We also talked about how there is a certain body type for dancers and how people will say to those who have the fit and slim body that they “look like a dancer”. Since when did we have to look a certain way to be considered a dancer?<br />
Another example is with my screening. Gayanne said that I am strong and that I do the right things for strength and stretching, and what she said I should focus on more is having more fun and not worrying so much about my body because I am already doing what I need to do for my body. I was so worried that she was gonna tell me that I need to work on everything on my body, but it was the opposite. It showed me how much I really put myself down. This screening caused me to be aware of my body overall, not just one part because I am now trying to have more fun with it and see what else my body can do. Discussing the screenings in class also showed how others worry about their bodies as well, and it is heartbreaking that we all have to worry about having our bodies be the “perfect dance body” when everybody is different and can never be “perfect”. I am still focused on getting my body more strong, toned, and flexible, but I want to show the world that you do not have to have a certain body type that has been plastered all across the dance world, and that every body type should be able to be accepted in the dance world. I realized this especially when we had to write down answers to questions in class like what makes our bodies beautiful? What makes them powerful? How we cherish them? And more. When I first got the paper I looked at it and sighed because I do not think my body is beautiful or powerful, but after I while I realized that it actually is. This is because I have full control of it, it can adapt and be taught to do new things or improve, it can create life, it is healthy, etc. I had to look deeper than just how I feel about my body and look at what it can do.<br />
(Theresa) Although I and Maya have different upbringings and personal histories we both can recognize that being a dancer has put a lot more of unwarranted pressure on our body and image. Constantly worried about how people might doubt us in the industry when it comes to this art form we now realize we just need to prove our artistry, hard work ethic, and passion to be successful. Although I didn’t have much trouble speaking out about my body in a positive manner, I understand how Maya and others in my class at first found it triggering. Hearing other people in my class’ stories about growing up in dance classes and feeling alienated because of their size, shape, or appearance was heartbreaking. However, this means that their love for dance is so strong that they endured all of these hardships to do what they love. As we get older and understand more, I hope dancers, dance teachers, and choreographers alike will recognize body positivity and the beauty of all bodies in a space. It is now our job to go forward in life as global citizens and advocates for self-love, body positivity, and confidence in oneself, although this can be challenging, it is so important.<br />
(Maya) The dance figure we decided to research on for this prompt is Savion Glover. Glover is an African American male tap dancer, known for his “quick steps and amazing rhythms”(Glover). According to Gregory Hines, tap legend and Glover’s former tap teacher, &#8220;Savion likes to start his pieces with some old school moves from famous tappers and then work his way into his own style. Hines says it’s like paying homage to those he respects”(Hines). This is also mentioned in an article titled Glover on the Buckrail website. It mentions how he sees it is his duty to carry the history of the greats before him while keeping the form modern and relevant. He uses his body as a vessel that contains the past and showcases what came before him and showing how it has evolved into the modern style of the form. To show the importance of knowing what came before, and how it caused tap to become what it is in the present day.<br />
Glover can be related to William Henry Lane, who was also known as The Master Juba, the inventor of tap. They can relate to each other because Juba used his body to create an amazing art form, and Glover is taking that history and showing it in present day. Especially with the way he dances because, like Juba did, he dances flat heeled, which is mentioned in his biography on The Library of Congress website.<br />
The fact that he uses his body as a tool for spreading awareness of history and its evolution reminds me of how I think about how I want to use my body to show that dancers do not need to have a specific body to be able to do what they love, which has recently become more aware in the dance world. Our bodies are our vessels, and everyone was gifted a different one. It has become more clear that people should love their bodies, and that representation is important, especially when it comes to the media because kids look to it too and it causes them to want to look like the people that are showcased.</p>
