			<div id="post-1496" class="post-1496 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-admissions-blog">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2012/11/weve-been-on-the-run-driving-in-the-sun-looking-out-for-number-one/" rel="bookmark">We&#8217;ve been on the run, Driving in the sun, Looking out for number one&#8230;.</a></h2>

			<div class="entry-meta">
				<span class="meta-prep meta-prep-author">Posted on</span> <a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2012/11/weve-been-on-the-run-driving-in-the-sun-looking-out-for-number-one/" title="11:43 am" rel="bookmark"><span class="entry-date">November 15, 2012</span></a> <span class="meta-sep">by</span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/author/czumberge/" title="View all posts by czumberge">czumberge</a></span>			</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

					<div class="entry-content">
				<p>So because of the research I did in nuclear physics this summer, I had the opportunity to go to the American Physics Society, Department of Nuclear Physics conference in beautiful Newport Beach, California a few weeks ago. I, along with my research professor and two other students that I worked with, flew out on a Tuesday afternoon.</p>
<p><a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/60c103c01d5011e2b71422000a1e9f9b_7.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1497 aligncenter" src="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/60c103c01d5011e2b71422000a1e9f9b_7-300x300.jpg" alt="Newark Airport Terminal to Orange County" width="300" height="300" srcset="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/60c103c01d5011e2b71422000a1e9f9b_7-300x300.jpg 300w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/60c103c01d5011e2b71422000a1e9f9b_7-150x150.jpg 150w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/60c103c01d5011e2b71422000a1e9f9b_7.jpg 612w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a></p>
<p>When I finally got off the six hour flight, it was only 10pm in California, but that was 1 a.m. back home, so as soon as I got checked into the hotel I went to bed.</p>
<p>The next day, we had no sessions that we had to attend, so the two other students and I joined up with a few kids from other universities and we went straight to the beach!</p>
<p><a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/59e30c601e0411e2b0f022000a9f1369_7.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1498 aligncenter" src="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/59e30c601e0411e2b0f022000a9f1369_7-300x300.jpg" alt="Pacific Ocean Beach in Orange County" width="300" height="300" srcset="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/59e30c601e0411e2b0f022000a9f1369_7-300x300.jpg 300w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/59e30c601e0411e2b0f022000a9f1369_7-150x150.jpg 150w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/59e30c601e0411e2b0f022000a9f1369_7.jpg 612w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a></p>
<p><a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/b57fa4f41e0c11e282c522000a1fa433_7.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1500 aligncenter" src="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/b57fa4f41e0c11e282c522000a1fa433_7-300x300.jpg" alt="Beach, take two!" width="300" height="300" srcset="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/b57fa4f41e0c11e282c522000a1fa433_7-300x300.jpg 300w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/b57fa4f41e0c11e282c522000a1fa433_7-150x150.jpg 150w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/b57fa4f41e0c11e282c522000a1fa433_7.jpg 612w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a></p>
<p>The water was freezing cold, but the weather was absolutely beautiful. The entire time we were out, there wasn&#8217;t a single cloud in the sky. The hotel itself was incredible. There were tons of plants and palm trees. Here was the view from our room:</p>
<p><a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/5372248a1dee11e2ab5722000a1f9684_7.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1499 aligncenter" src="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/5372248a1dee11e2ab5722000a1f9684_7-300x300.jpg" alt="The view from our hotel room" width="300" height="300" srcset="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/5372248a1dee11e2ab5722000a1f9684_7-300x300.jpg 300w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/5372248a1dee11e2ab5722000a1f9684_7-150x150.jpg 150w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/5372248a1dee11e2ab5722000a1f9684_7.jpg 612w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a></p>
<p>The conference itself was an incredible, unique experience. Every day, I had the opportunity to sit in on talks and workshops from people who are the expects in their field from all over the United States. Most of the talks were on the cutting edge research that&#8217;s occurring now and types of research that will be occurring when we undergraduates enter the post graduate field.</p>
<p>The most important event, and the reason we flew out, was the undergraduate poster fair. This was where we undergrads presented posters of our summer research and professionals, graduate students, professors and post-doctorate researchers would walk around and ask you questions about your research.</p>
<p><a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/78.jpeg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1501 aligncenter" src="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/78-300x225.jpeg" alt="Conference Experience for Undergraduate Poster Fair" width="300" height="225" srcset="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/78-300x225.jpeg 300w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/78.jpeg 1024w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a></p>
<p>During the poster fair, I met a ton of people from some places I hadn&#8217;t even heard of, and I had the opportunity to make contacts in the physics world that I probably wouldn&#8217;t have ever talked to on my own. And they&#8217;re all doing their own interesting research, some in topics I have never come across in any of my studies.</p>
<p>In total, there were about 120 undergraduate students there from across the US, and I made a few good friends out there from different universities, friends who I still chat with now and bounce ideas around with when we all inevitably reach a sticky spot in our respective researches.</p>
<p>This was definitely one of the greatest academic experiences that I have ever had.</p>
<p><a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/Untitled.png"><img class="alignleft size-large wp-image-1502" src="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/Untitled-1024x452.png" alt="All the Undergraduates Who Attended the Conference" width="640" height="282" srcset="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/Untitled-1024x452.png 1024w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/Untitled-300x132.png 300w, http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2012/11/Untitled.png 1483w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px" /></a></p>

			<div id="post-1486" class="post-1486 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-admissions-blog">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2012/11/lake-nakuru-in-retrospect/" rel="bookmark">Lake Nakuru in Retrospect</a></h2>

			<div class="entry-meta">
				<span class="meta-prep meta-prep-author">Posted on</span> <a href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2012/11/lake-nakuru-in-retrospect/" title="12:09 pm" rel="bookmark"><span class="entry-date">November 12, 2012</span></a> <span class="meta-sep">by</span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://admissions.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/author/edward/" title="View all posts by Edward">Edward</a></span>			</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

					<div class="entry-content">
				<p>My recent expedition into Lake Nakuru National Park was just that, an expedition. We spent 5 days and 4 night in the park, but that was after the 9-hour drive through city and countryside to get from Kilimanjaro Bush Camp to the lake.</p>
<p>The drive was definitely a fun part of the trip. We made some cool stops and it was fascinating to drive through Nairobi. It&#8217;s the image of a developing city. Skyscrapers and city buses wind through large patches of acacia trees and rocky outcroppings. It was a fascinating fusion of two very different kinds of wilderness, and I hope I get a chance to really explore the city at least once in my life.</p>
<p>We also discovered during the last hour or two of our drive that everyone in the car was a Harry Potter fan. Coincidentally, I am reading through all the books again on my Kindle and was in the midst of number four during this drive. Thus, I spent the last leg of our journey reading Harry Potter aloud to the others. I&#8217;m not sure if that casts doubts about our maturity or whether it confirms our adulthood, but it was awesome.</p>
<p>When we arrived at Lake Nakuru, we could tell immediately that it was going to be a very different experience than the other National Parks that, by now, we were pros at navigating and game driving.</p>
<div style="width: 614px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img src="https://fbcdn-sphotos-b-a.akamaihd.net/hphotos-ak-prn1/21777_4943189456387_1028807977_n.jpg" alt="" width="604" height="453" /><p class="wp-caption-text">The overlook from baboon cliff</p></div>
<p>First of all this was the only of a total 4 lakes that we&#8217;d been brought to which we could actual see had water, and oh did it have water. The region has experienced an inexplicable spike in rainfall and the water had swallowed its banks, several tree trunks and was threatening the roads winding through the park. It rained nearly every day, and let me just say, driving through the rain with the tops popped on our vehicles is nothing short of exhilarating.</p>
<p>Famous as a the first rhino sanctuary, Lake Nakuru was the first chance many of us had to really see rhinos up close and in abundance. The leathery African unicorns had previously eluded us in the Tanzanian parks, but here we had a whole assignment devoted to studying their behavior and time budgeting. Unlike in Serengeti, we were staying at what they called a hostel, which was actually a few huts and a main kitchen and dining hall. It was cool to experience living in a national park both in tents, like we did in Serengeti and in slightly more established structures in Lake Nakuru. (It was also awesome not to camp in the cold and pelting rain).</p>
<div style="width: 614px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img src="https://fbcdn-sphotos-g-a.akamaihd.net/hphotos-ak-prn1/150506_4943191456437_404758334_n.jpg" alt="" width="604" height="453" /><p class="wp-caption-text">A Rhino and her Babyno</p></div>
<p>One very unique aspect about this expedition was that rather than the wildlife, the biogeography and landscapes were the most stunning and beautiful aspects of the experience. Being surrounded by such natural beauty was really transformative in a now-familiar way that I don&#8217;t think I can explain. You&#8217;ve gotta feel it, and I don&#8217;t think I could even explain it to a past version of myself who hadn&#8217;t experienced it for himself. Case in point, everyone needs to spend a few days of their life with nature. I also had the first moment in my life where I actually got emotional about trees. Listening to a lecture of habitat degradation and invasive species, I found myself quite empathetic toward the dying forest.</p>
<div style="width: 614px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img src="https://fbcdn-sphotos-f-a.akamaihd.net/hphotos-ak-prn1/542868_4943188696368_1644210110_n.jpg" alt="" width="604" height="453" /><p class="wp-caption-text">The Mau Forest Catchment Zone</p></div>
<p>We rounded off the trip with a few hours at a luxurious lodge inside the park. We felt a little out of place when we clambered out of our land cruisers, dusty and safari-worn and were handed heavy tumblers of passionfruit juice and warm eucalyptus face cloths, but I can&#8217;t say I hated the special treatment. We lounged for a bit, resting our bodies, lunching on some comforts of the states (cheeseburgers and steak anyone?) and having a couple of drinks while it stormed outside. The lodge had on CNN so that we could keep up with the election news.</p>
<p>Speaking of which, it was certainly an odd feeling to spend the first presidential election for which I was of legal voting age in a country on the other side of the world. That being said, it wasn&#8217;t as though the Kenyans weren&#8217;t invested in the election. Radio stations, television and newspapers followed the election closely. Obama&#8217;s father hails from a Kenyan tribe in the area. In fact, one of the staff that was on expedition with us came from that same tribe.</p>
<p>On our way back we stopped at an elephant orphanage and re-introduction program. I have only two words for that: Baby. Elephants.</p>
<p>It was a sweet experience, and I&#8217;m glad I got to have it. Soon we Mmarekani here at KBC are going to be buckling down and charging headfirst into our directed research projects. Hopefully my head will be above the tide of data and analysis long enough for me to keep ya&#8217;ll updated. Until then, kwa heri and kuwa huru!</p>
