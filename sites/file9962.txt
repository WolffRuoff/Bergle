			<p align="center">A Monthly Update<br />
8/4/10</p>
<p><em><strong>Coffee and Copyright: &#8220;Who Own the Rights to My Work?&#8221;</strong></em></p>
<blockquote><p>If you have authored or will author anything, mark your calendar for Wednesday Nov. 3 at 4:30 p.m. Kelly Cannon, Scholarly Communication Librarian, will lead a discussion in the library on what your rights are to your existing publications, and how to negotiate rights to your publications going forward.</p></blockquote>
<p><em><strong>Good News about the Use of Film Clips in the Classroom</strong></em></p>
<blockquote><p>A new exemption in copyright law allows</p>
<blockquote><p>a)professors in all areas of study,<br />
and<br />
b) professors and students in film and media studies</p></blockquote>
<p>to hack encrypted DVDs and clip &#8220;short portions&#8221; for educational purposes.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.insidehighered.com/news/2010/07/28/copyright">Inside Higher Ed</a> comments: &#8220;If the words &#8216;sweeping new exemptions to the anti-circumvention provisions of the Digital Millenium Copyright Act&#8217; make you want whoop for joy and join a conga line, you just might be a <a href="http://www.copyright.gov/fls/fl102.html">fair use</a> advocate &#8211; one who wants professors and students to be able to decrypt and excerpt copyrighted video content for lectures and class projects. Since Monday [July 26], a lot of advocates have been dancing.</p>
<p>&#8220;&#8216;This is very exciting,&#8217; says Patricia Aufderheide, a communications professor and director of the Center for Social Media at American University. &#8216;We&#8217;re doing nothing but chat about this, we&#8217;re so excited.'&#8221;</p></blockquote>
<p><em><strong>Infomaniac Tip</strong></em><br />
by Jen Jarson, Information Literacy and Assessment Librarian</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>Visualize!</strong></p>
<p>Looking for visual representations of text? Try Wordle&#8230;</p>
<p><a href="http://www.wordle.net/">Wordle</a> &#8220;is a toy for generating &#8216;word clouds&#8217; from text that you provide. The clouds give greater prominence to words that appear more frequently in the source text. You can tweak your clouds with different fonts, layouts, and color schemes. The images you create with Wordle are yours to use however you like. You can print them out, or save them to the Wordle gallery to share with your friends.&#8221;</p>
<p>Word clouds are not only visually interesting, but can also help users examine major themes and issues in a text, identify key vocabulary, reveal overused words in a user&#8217;s own writing, and more.</p>
<p>See, for example, word clouds of James Joyce&#8217;s <a href="http://www.wordle.net/show/wrdl/2255365/A_Portrait_of_the_Artist_as_a_Young_Man">A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man</a> or the <a href="http://www.wordle.net/show/wrdl/1381018/US_Constitution">U.S. Constitution</a>. Browse the Wordle gallery or create your own at <a href="http://www.wordle.net/">http://www.wordle.net/</a>.</p></blockquote>
