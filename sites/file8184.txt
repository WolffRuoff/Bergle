			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f15.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2015/09/22/extinction-of-newspapers/" rel="bookmark">Extinction of Newspapers</a></h2>

			<div class="entry-meta">
				<span class="meta-prep meta-prep-author">Posted on</span> <a href="http://fys142f15.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2015/09/22/extinction-of-newspapers/" title="7:49 pm" rel="bookmark"><span class="entry-date">September 22, 2015</span></a> <span class="meta-sep">by</span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://fys142f15.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/author/jsaperstein/" title="View all posts by Jesse Saperstein">Jesse Saperstein</a></span>			</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

					<div class="entry-content">
				<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Before apps and the internet, people needed to wait to get their news. Newspapers were delivered early in the morning packed front to back with the latest news from the day before. Aside from the nightly news, newspapers were the main way to know what was going on in the world. Mornings were spent reading newspaper articles at the breakfast table while sipping coffee and eating breakfast. Fast forward to the 21st century where people rely on apps such as Twitter to get breaking information at the palm of their hands instead of waiting for the paper boy to deliver the newspaper every morning. With an increasing reliability on apps that provide breaking news whenever and wherever, the use of newspapers is at an all time low and continuing to decline. According to research done by The Future Exploration Network, “Newspapers in their current form will become insignificant in the United States by 2017,” (Gunelius, 2014). The Future Exploration Network’s research has the United States as the first country to forgo newspapers. Soon after their 2017 prediction for the United States, nations such as Iceland, Canada, Norway, England, and Finland will all follow the United States in eliminating newspapers in current form. Gunelius states that the end is definitely approaching for newspapers, “Unless newspapers can find a way to transform their business models, Future Exploration Network’s extinction predictions for newspapers as we know them could come to fruition even sooner. Research keeps showing again and again that the end is near. How soon the end will arrive has yet to be determined,”(Gunelius,2014). </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">With the extinction of newspapers in their current paper form on the way, no one is able to deny that the world continues to become more and more digital by the day. As I read Gunelius’ article, I thought of how books have gone digital with the Kindle and the ability to download books straight to your smartphone. Schools are starting to use Ipads and and other tablets for reading books instead of having physical copies for students. I believe that books in their current form will not be available in a matter of years just as The Future Exploration Network predicted for newspapers. Huge bookstore chains such as Barnes and Noble have had a huge increase in store closures as a result of the increase in digital purchases of books.  With the digital world becoming increasingly widespread every single day, physical copies of newspapers, books, magazines and other paper produced materials have their days limited.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f15.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2015/09/21/facebook-is-far-from-giving-up/" rel="bookmark">Facebook is far from giving up</a></h2>

			<div class="entry-meta">
				<span class="meta-prep meta-prep-author">Posted on</span> <a href="http://fys142f15.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2015/09/21/facebook-is-far-from-giving-up/" title="8:34 pm" rel="bookmark"><span class="entry-date">September 21, 2015</span></a> <span class="meta-sep">by</span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://fys142f15.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/author/dd251040/" title="View all posts by Doug D">Doug D</a></span>			</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

					<div class="entry-content">
				<p>With the release of the tool “Signal,” social media tech giant Facebook looks to help journalists discover new and spreading content. With the use of social media on the rise, inefficiency comes with it. While sites such as Facebook, Twitter and Instagram sit back and enjoy all that’s taking place, social media followers may begin to feel a sense of overcrowding. Posts, happenings, and pictures are just some of the things that typically flood our newsfeeds. But is it all meaningful? Numerous analysts have observed that skipping around and scrolling through feeds trying to find the right post is becoming more common. So what? It’s extremely time consuming and news organizations aren’t receiving the credit they deserve. It has become a hunt and find for many and with all the new ways to share, it can become a bit overwhelming to lots. Facebook wants to change this.</p>
<p>In addition to communicating and sharing with friends, many see Facebook as a place to keep up on current news topics. According to Greenberg, (2015, p. 1) Signal offers a dashboard where journalists can follow trending and emerging topics, track chatter around public figures on a leaderboard, and search to find public conversations, photos, or videos. All in one place. One may be wondering where users seek new stories. Facebook made it clear that users can source articles via hashtags from anywhere on its social media site. I strongly believe that this will benefit both, Facebook users in addition to journalists. When I found out the feature was arriving now, I was blown away.</p>
<p>Facebook has been working endlessly to further lift their strengths and improve upon their weaknesses. Most of them being related to journalists. Just this past week, Facebook gave journalists the permission to live broadcast video using the company’s Mention application. This enhancement takes it not only a step forward, but an entire leap in the right direction. I strongly believe companies such as Facebook who are constantly working to improve and always rolling out updates deserve their place in the market. After reading some of Facebook’s stats in class along with other social media engines, it’s clear that Facebook is on par with competitors and its growth in the past few years have left me in disbelief. This just further exemplifies how much technology is expanding.</p>
<p>The following line is essential to the overall meaning of the article. “Signal is more than just another way for journalists to use Facebook. It’s a way for Facebook to show off the value of Facebook.” (Greenberg, 2015) But, why is this important? This statement sums up Facebook’s goal. Greenberg was absolutely right and makes a valid claim. Signal brings a whole new experience to both Facebook users and journalists. It will increase the value of published articles, make them easier to find, and help journalists predict upcoming trends as well as hot new topics. After all, news is an essential piece of the experience that comes on Facebook. I believe this will fill lots of hopes and further establish who Facebook really is and why they set out to exist. Not only will it help Facebook and news organizations provide relevant topics to millions of users, but it will push the limits of the industry to do the same. And that’s what keeps the world moving forward.</p>
<p>Greenberg, J. (2015, September 17). Facebook Tries to Lure Journalists Away From Twitter. Retrieved from <a href="http://www.wired.com/2015/09/facebook-gives-journalists-new-way-find-news-facebook/">http://www.wired.com/2015/09/facebook-gives-journalists-new-way-find-news-facebook/</a></p>

			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f15.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2015/09/20/the-dangers-of-the-internet/" rel="bookmark">The Dangers of the Internet</a></h2>

			<div class="entry-meta">
				<span class="meta-prep meta-prep-author">Posted on</span> <a href="http://fys142f15.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2015/09/20/the-dangers-of-the-internet/" title="6:45 pm" rel="bookmark"><span class="entry-date">September 20, 2015</span></a> <span class="meta-sep">by</span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://fys142f15.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/author/willlerrick/" title="View all posts by Will Lerrick">Will Lerrick</a></span>			</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

					<div class="entry-content">
				<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Cyber Bullying and Facebook</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Social media has taken up a huge part of our daily lives. Without it we feel lost, unconnected with society, and left out. We have almost become completely dependent on it. I couldn&#8217;t imagine a day going without facebook, and many other people couldn&#8217;t either. We spend so much time online and meet so many new people through social networking it’s hard to tell who’s who online.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">The article I read, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400">Cyberbullying on Facebook, How to Teach Teens About it?</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400"> Explains how the majority of our younger generation spends their time interacting online, especially on social media. The author explains the dangers of social media and the stigma associated with it. With all this time interacting online comes the danger of cyber bullying. People do not realize how prevalent and easy it is for someone to be bullied online. Bullies look to the internet and social media because it is easier to hurt someone online when there is an added anonymity factor. Bullies feel like it’s okay to bully other people online because they think it won’t hurt the other person. The author writes, “Cyber bullying takes behaviour born in the schoolyard and adds two elements: ubiquity and anonymity.” This is a very scary concept because what the author is saying is that a bully can be anywhere and anyone when you’re online. You never really know who is who online and you can never be sure that who you meet online is actually that person in real life.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">One main theme that comes up throughout the article is how people choose to behave online. The author writes, “Words that appeared frequently included “rude,” “mean,” “fake,” “crude,” “over-dramatic,” and “disrespectful.”’ I believe the sole reason that people are unkind online is anonymity. People feel as if there is a shield blocking them from the outside world. That is not true. Everything you say online stays there. Even when you think something is deleted, chances are it’s still there. Something you say online can hurt someone just as much if not more than if you said it to them in person. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">A lot of people choose to act differently online because they feel like they can be someone else and change who they are. The author writes, “Often teens felt bolder, ruder, or more empowered </span><a href="http://nobullying.com/cyber-safety-facebook-and-real-life-the-struggle/"><span style="font-weight: 400">because they did not fear physical violence in the online space</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400">.” The author also writes, “More often than not, teens believe that they find friends and romantic interests much easier to talk to and way more open in a Facebook chat conversation than in a playground or classroom context.” Both these quotes are related by anonymity. The fact that you can talk to someone (and possibly harm them as well) without being face to face with them. Some people may feel more comfortable talking online then in person.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Cyber bullying is connected with facebook in many ways, one of them being its popularity. So many people have access to facebook and it’s so easy to find people that it might be a cyber bully’s first place to look. The author says, “It can lead to the end of friendships, problems with parents, or wanting to avoid school. According to pewinternet.org, 88% of social media-using teens have seen someone be mean or cruel on Facebook.” 88% is a huge number of people who witness cyberbullying. Think of how large that number would be if private messaging didn’t exist and everything was public. That is just one of the reasons cyberbullying on Facebook is so prevalent.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">But cyber bullying isn&#8217;t just exclusive to facebook or any other type of social media. The author writes, “There has been considerable concern among parents, teachers, policy makers, and advocates about the nature and intensity of online social encounters among teens. The majority of teens have positive online experiences, but some are caught in an online feedback loop of meanness and negative experiences.” Cyber bullying can happen anywhere, to anyone, and at anytime. The dangers of social media will always be present as long as people continue to use it. Overall, I agree with everything the author says. Cyber bullying is a problem. I don’t think there is a way to stop it. I believe the only way to limit it, is to educate children about cyberbullying and the dangers of social media when used improperly.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
