<p>For the past nine years, the library has recognized students who demonstrate growth in information literacy through independent study or research. &nbsp;Students are nominated by faculty and the award winners are chosen through an application and review process.&nbsp; This year’s winners are&nbsp;Jona Lieberman &#8217;20, nominated by Grant Scott, and Anthony Fillis &#8217;19, nominated by Irene Chien. </p>



<p>Anthony&#8217;s research focused on&nbsp;virtual reality in education, which resulted in a more nuanced understanding of the roles of VR&nbsp;affordances in relation to accessibility, and what exactly is “successful” VR. </p>



<p>
Anthony wrote:  

</p>



<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="alignright is-resized"><img loading="lazy" src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/m2dz3gujdUbu6YVs-m6PGT27IbBV76A4psy9pLoq4yvUTtGvU5VcHNByX2MyuGY0dEL8Y4O7gxW1wZve2pbDzc8Mbu3PRiw6HIAxJXvN3pxwlSHJPCZJwq-a8BV7yVobTkXNN6lbcvf40bO-zQ" alt="C:\Users\Jessica\Downloads\IMG_7439.jpg" width="381" height="286"/><figcaption>Anthony Fillis, &#8217;19</figcaption></figure></div>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>My research was an exploration of virtual reality (VR) in education. . .the idea of accessibility in VR education emerged as a vital aspect of educational affordance theory that was largely overlooked. By comparing the discourses, I was able to identify points of friction between the two on accessibility. This friction pointed to the multi-faceted nature of accessibility that was largely undocumented. . . However, both accessibility discourses hold gaps and overlook certain facets of access. Therefore, I called for educators to address the following: accessibility of cost, disabled access, mismatching users skill with the hardware/software, and shifting students from consumers of educational technology to producers of content. This research resulted in a more nuanced understanding of the roles of VR affordances in relation to accessibility, and what exactly is “successful” VR.<br></p></blockquote>



<p>Anthony&#8217;s description of scholarly communication set him apart.  </p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p> . . .Prior to this deep dive in VR education research, I knew that I had something to say about VR and accessibility. However, establishing a true level of immersion in the scholarly text was what enabled me to see scholarship as a conversation. This information conversation held gaps, which I found myself ready and able to fill due to the iterative and back and forth nature of my inquiry. The scholarship became very familiar to me, allowing me to find the authoritative scholars in the conversation. I was able to see their web of influence and citation across the rest of the discourse, finding moments of other scholars challenging and adding onto the authoritative and dominant ideas in the field. However, with my specific style of inquiry, which weaves scholarly and industrial sources, I was able to bring a new perspective to the conversation and shine light on aspects that were largely overlooked, or left as an implicit idea, rather than being made prominent and explicit in their conversation. . .</p></blockquote>



<p>

Jona&#8217;s project involved the Martin Fetherolf WWI memoir housed in Special Collections, bringing his memoir to life and making it visually accessible by incorporating her research into Storymap, an interactive mapping tool.&nbsp;

</p>



<p>Jona wrote,</p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p> My independent study focused on the memoir of Martin Fetherolf, a 1914 Muhlenberg College graduate who served overseas during World War I as a supply man. He spent several years in France during the war before returning to Allentown. The goal of my independent study was to bring to life his memoir and to make it accessible as a more visual medium. . .In addition to mapping where he went, I also added in historical information to clarify certain aspects of his journal and found photos that helped bring his words to life.</p></blockquote>



<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="alignleft is-resized"><img data-attachment-id="2511" data-permalink="http://trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/jona-lieberman-2019/" data-orig-file="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/jona-lieberman-2019-e1557503520225.jpg?fit=457%2C608" data-orig-size="457,608" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-meta="{&quot;aperture&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;credit&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;camera&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;caption&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;created_timestamp&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;copyright&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;focal_length&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;iso&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;shutter_speed&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;title&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;orientation&quot;:&quot;0&quot;}" data-image-title="jona-lieberman-2019" data-image-description="" data-medium-file="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/jona-lieberman-2019-e1557503520225.jpg?fit=225%2C300" data-large-file="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/jona-lieberman-2019-e1557503520225.jpg?fit=457%2C608" loading="lazy" src="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/jona-lieberman-2019-e1557503520225.jpg?resize=249%2C332" alt="" class="wp-image-2511" width="249" height="332" srcset="https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/jona-lieberman-2019-e1557503520225.jpg?w=457 457w, https://i0.wp.com/trexlerworks.muhlenberg.edu/trexler_blog/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/jona-lieberman-2019-e1557503520225.jpg?resize=225%2C300 225w" sizes="(max-width: 249px) 100vw, 249px" data-recalc-dims="1" /><figcaption>Jona Lieberman, &#8217;20</figcaption></figure></div>



<p>Jona emphasized the choices that she made in her research process, impressing the committee with her awareness that these choices affect the final product.</p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>The concepts that resonate with me the most from my independent study are the idea of information creation as a process and scholarship as a conversation. When I first started the independent study and the research component, I did a lot of reading. I began with Fetherolf’s memoir, but then moved to other first-hand accounts of World War I, and then finally to secondary sources. . .The memoir of Fetherolf chronicles his day to day life during the war and rarely talks about specific strategies . . .Due to the fact that I was focusing on his experience, and his memoir, I chose to not follow up on the strategy element in exploring World War I. My conscious choice to exclude that line of thinking shows how the information creation process is rooted in acts of exclusion. . .Who gave me the authority to make those decisions? Frankly, no one did explicitly. However, because I had chosen to follow Fetherolf’s journey and I had been researching for months, I felt that I had enough information behind me to begin to make that type of decision. . . By purposely choosing to exclude information, I brought Fetherolf’s memories to the forefront of this project and I hope I paid homage to his experiences.</p></blockquote>

<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="alignright is-resized"><img loading="lazy" src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/m_46n8vN0_e2lRKs8fN5vIdahSpV5-ktLi3EaWn3zIpGKe4yJmZfqIE6WcubzPwtkyI8QLySZoI8wSlWgV0EE2SRwRWhTHxtJcdVjMYPShCNbkrXOO0RFGHb3SnscPGPU6POMWd9eNlMfsR7fQ" alt="K:\PHOTOGRAPHS\Novemberfest\IMG_1619.JPG" width="160" height="213"/><figcaption>Off With Her Head!<br><strong>Novemberfest 2010</strong></figcaption></figure></div>



<p>In 2010 Trexler Library’s staff was given a challenge. &nbsp;Make the library  a place for  more than study time and books. &nbsp;Let’s make use of the  space.  Let’s put some fun in the library. &nbsp;Make it a destination.  The  staff from all departments of the library took up the challenge and  worked together on the first event –  Novemberfest 2010. &nbsp;We turned   Level A of the library into an arcade.   Multiple video games were set up  around the floor as well as carnival  and board games, and a fortune  telling station. WMUH was on hand with  music for the event. &nbsp;At the end  of the evening, fun had been had but  the event needed almost as many  people to work the games as played  them.  We could do better!  </p>



<p> </p>



<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="alignleft is-resized"><img loading="lazy" src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/rKmxOhw3rpzdzc4_tLPVX_e06wltMdWK7KUQveAjGsPpx2iTEZwkpSMo-aC0Fq0LKB-OsFaUKf3koGg5EVr8fKos7XF5_OJP3Q8UkhBLQrqa4ctxGAQJwMxJ0MJNN78Jd9g1nLhAIN1F4NnAPA" alt="" width="369" height="208"/></figure></div>



<p>Tim Clark, who was the Systems Librarian at the time, recommended hosting a trivia event. &nbsp;He organized his team and the planning began. &nbsp;Ninety minutes of trivia, snacks, and a grand prize basket for the highest scoring team. &nbsp;The library staff approached local businesses for prizes, and began marketing the event. Tim’s confidence waned a bit as the evening approached, and he offered to eat his left shoe if we made our goal of 10 teams. &nbsp;On February 16, 2011, Trexler Library held its first Trivia Night. &nbsp;Twelve teams were in attendance…we’re still waiting for Tim to eat that shoe!  </p>



<p> </p>



<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="alignright is-resized"><img loading="lazy" src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/lYmf5NfrQ8-9VOZV7dPHEs1oqyz3h04p9zNYio-i8W0SPDNF197oA6YzjB7fOs7KPxkf-ap4LoXJiy0oedWHf12dZHs0bMVccu6lUVVMLsJWXQUzBLPdi7i1kLv0itnhNerOTE7m3IsxHvG-jw" alt="K:\PHOTOGRAPHS\Trivia Night\Trivia Night (Spring 2017)\Trexler Library Presents, Jurassic Trivia 045.JPG" width="416" height="312"/><figcaption>Jurassic Trivia, Spring 2017</figcaption></figure></div>



<p style="text-align:left">Now held twice per year, the staff at Trexler Library continually work to improve Trivia Night.  New rounds are developed for each event, themes have been incorporated, decorations have escalated, and guest M.C.’s are garnered from various academic and business departments across campus, each adding their personalities and fun side to the evening.  Some past themes have inspired students to come in costume such as Halloween, Monster Mash, and Red Carpet Trivia. Other memorable evenings have included Inauguration Trivia, which was held during President Williams Inaugural weekend, and Jurassic Trivia which we promoted with a short video (<em><a href="https://www.facebook.com/muhlenbergtrexlerlibrary/videos/10158120838565621/">check it out on our Facebook page</a></em>).  </p>



<p>Trivia Night attracts between 80 and 130 participants with the Spring semester being the more popular. Teams of 3-6 members work together to achieve the highest score. &nbsp;Each member of the three highest scoring teams win Amazon Gift cards. &nbsp;There are also opportunities for individuals to win prizes during the evening.  Trivia Night is open to the Muhlenberg Community, which includes students, faculty and staff. &nbsp;</p>



<p>We hope to see you at our next Trivia Night on Monday, February 11<sup>th</sup>, when Dr. Erika Iyengar leads us in <em><a href="https://www.facebook.com/events/2197873917129976/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener" aria-label=" (opens in a new tab)">Trivia From</a></em><a href="https://www.facebook.com/events/2197873917129976/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener" aria-label=" (opens in a new tab)"> </a><em><a href="https://www.facebook.com/events/2197873917129976/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener" aria-label=" (opens in a new tab)">The Deep</a></em>. </p>

<h2><b><u><i>Timeline of Muhlenberg Visitors Goes Live</i></u></b></h2>
<div>Beginning with leaders of Lutheran thought in the days when the College was primarily an institution for the training of young men bound for the ministry, speakers from the neighboring region and beyond have come to share their learning and opinions with students, faculty, and staff.</div>
<div></div>
<div><a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/library/digital-exhibits/muhlenberg-visitors-timeline/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">This timeline</a> seeks to catalog and describe those visiting speakers from 1900 to present. The timeline was prepared by Maddux Pearson, Class of 2018, during an internship in Trexler Library Special Collections and Archives in the 2017-2018 academic year. His research was conducted primarily using the Muhlenberg Weekly and the Ciarla, as well as other historical College materials available in the archives.</div>
