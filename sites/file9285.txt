			<div id="s-lg-content-33974485" class="  clearfix">
				<p><strong>WHAT IS A SCHOLARLY SOURCE?</strong></p>
<p>Scholarly sources are in-depth, research-based articles, books, and book chapters, written by scholars and experts and intended for other researchers, experts, and students.The following characteristics can identify a source as scholarly:</p>
<ul>
<li><em>AUTHOR</em><br />The author of the source is a scholar or expert in the field discussed, not just a journalist or "staff writer."<br /><br /></li>
<li><em>CITED SOURCES</em><br />The source provides substantial citations, such as footnotes or a bibliography.<br /><br /></li>
<li><em>CONTENT &amp; PURPOSE</em><br />The source discusses a narrowly defined topic and may review existing literature concerning the topic, present new research based on experiments or archival work, or interpret a well-researched idea in a new way.<br /><br /></li>
<li><em>LANGUAGE &amp; AUDIENCE</em><br />The article uses technical language specific to the discipline, often requiring the reader to have scholarly background in the area.&nbsp; The source is intended for scholars, researchers, and students, rather than the general public.<br /><br /></li>
<li><em>PEER REVIEW</em><br />The source is reviewed and edited by other experts in the discipline.</li>
</ul>
<p></p>
<p><strong>WHY USE SCHOLARLY SOURCES?</strong></p>
<p>Because scholarly sources are well-researched, written by experts, and discuss topics in great depth and detail, they have credibility and authority.&nbsp; Using scholarly sources in your own research gives your work credibility and authority, as well.&nbsp; Non-scholarly materials can also be useful and appropriate for some academic purposes, but generally scholarly sources are expected in academic research.</p>
<p></p>
<p><strong>WHAT IS A PEER-REVIEWED SOURCE?</strong></p>
<p>Peer review is a process through which some scholarly articles and book chapters are critically evaluated by experts with in-depth knowledge of the research area before publication in order to ensure the information is valid, credible, well-written, and of high quality<span>.</span></p>
<p><span></span>Many databases will tell you if an article has been peer-reviewed.&nbsp; You may even be able to use limits or filters in the database to narrow your search results. A journal's website will often describe whether or not they use peer review. If you're still not sure if an article has been peer-reviewed, search the name of the journal in which the article was published in <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://ulrichsweb.serialssolutions.com/">Ulrichsweb Periodicals Directory</a>.&nbsp; The symbol of a referee's jersey next to the journal name indicates the journal uses peer review.&nbsp; (Refereed is a synonym for peer reviewed.)&nbsp; Note, though, that not all content in peer-reviewed journals is actually peer-reviewed; book or film reviews, letters to the editors, opinion columns, and articles without references are typically not peer-reviewed.</p>
<p></p>
<p><strong>WHAT IS AN EMPIRICAL SOURCE?</strong></p>
<p>Empirical sources are those that report on original experiments or observations. To identify a source as empirical, look for descriptions of the methodology of study. The source generally will include three notable sections: a section labeled "methods," "methodology," or "research design" that details how the study was designed and conducted; a section labeled "results" or "findings" that describes what the researchers learned in the study; and a section labeled "discussion" or "conclusion" that interprets the data gathered and describes the importance and impact of the study's findings.</p>

							<div id="s-lg-box-collapse-15574500" >
								<div class="s-lib-box-content">
									
			<div id="s-lg-content-33973607" class="  clearfix">
				<p>Plagiarism is the act of using another person's words or ideas without giving him/her proper credit.&nbsp; It is a serious academic offense.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The <a href="http://muhlenberg.edu/main/aboutus/dean-academic/integrity/">Academic Integrity</a> page defines plagiarism in more detail and provides information about Muhlenberg's Academic Integrity Code. </p>
<p>Indiana University's plagiarism tutorial also offers helpful information on understanding and recognizing plagiarism, including an <a href="https://www.indiana.edu/~istd/overview.html">overview</a> with basics and recommendations and <a href="https://www.indiana.edu/~istd/examples.html">examples</a><b>. &nbsp;</b></p>
