          <h1 class="entry-title ">NYTIMES &#8211; Lower Back Ache? Be Active and Wait It Out, New Guidelines Say</h1><div class="entry-meta"><a href="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?m=20170216" title="2:36 pm" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date updated" datetime="16 Feb, 2017">February 16, 2017</time></a> &nbsp;in <a class="" href="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?cat=1" title="View all posts in Uncategorized"> Uncategorized </a> <span class="by-author"> by <span class="author vcard author_name"><a class="url fn n" href="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?author=846" title="View all posts by Emma Jahde" rel="author">Emma Jahde</a></span></span> </div><hr class="featurette-divider __before_content">        </header>
                  <section class="entry-content ">
              <header id="story-header" class="story-header">
<div id="story-meta" class="story-meta ">
<div id="story-meta-footer" class="story-meta-footer">
<p class="byline-dateline"><a href="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2017/02/lower-back.jpg" class="grouped_elements" rel="tc-fancybox-group1469"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1470" src="http://bergdance.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2017/02/lower-back.jpg" alt="lower back" width="259" height="194" /></a></p>
<p class="byline-dateline"><span class="byline">By <span class="byline-author" data-byline-name="GINA KOLATA" data-twitter-handle="ginakolata"><a title="More Articles by GINA KOLATA" href="https://www.nytimes.com/by/gina-kolata">GINA KOLATA</a> </span></span><time class="dateline" datetime="2017-02-15T15:25:06-05:00">FEB. 13, 2017</time></p>
<p class="byline-dateline">NYTIMES</p>
</div>
</div>
</header>
<div class="story-body-supplemental">
<div class="story-body story-body-1">
<figure id="media-100000004928055" class="media photo lede layout-large-horizontal" data-media-action="modal">
<div class="image">
<p><img class="media-viewer-candidate" src="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/02/13/science/14SPINE1/14SPINE1-master768.jpg" alt="" data-mediaviewer-src="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/02/13/science/14SPINE1/14SPINE1-superJumbo.jpg" data-mediaviewer-caption="Sommer Kleweno Walley at home in Seattle. After she slipped on steps and began having back pain, a doctor prescribed physical therapy and an anti-inflammatory drug. Her pain was gone in a few months." data-mediaviewer-credit="Ian C. Bates for The New York Times" /></p>
<div class="media-action-overlay"></div>
</div><figcaption class="caption"><span class="caption-text">Sommer Kleweno Walley at home in Seattle. After she slipped on steps and began having back pain, a doctor prescribed physical therapy and an anti-inflammatory drug. Her pain was gone in a few months. </span><span class="credit"><span class="visually-hidden">Credit </span>Ian C. Bates for The New York Times</span></figcaption></figure>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="208" data-total-count="208">Dr. James Weinstein, a back pain specialist and chief executive of Dartmouth-Hitchcock Health System, has some advice for most people with <a class="meta-classifier" title="In-depth reference and news articles about Back pain - low." href="http://health.nytimes.com/health/guides/symptoms/back-pain-low/overview.html?inline=nyt-classifier">lower back pain</a>: Take two aspirin and don’t call me in the morning.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="318" data-total-count="526">On Monday, the <a href="https://www.acponline.org/">American College of Physicians</a> published updated <a href="http://annals.org/aim/article/doi/10.7326/M16-2367">guidelines</a> that say much the same. In making the new recommendations for the treatment of most people with lower back pain, the group is bucking what many doctors do and changing its previous guidelines, which called for medication as first-line therapy.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="297" data-total-count="823">Dr. Nitin Damle, president of the group’s board of regents and a practicing internist, said pills, even over-the-counter pain relievers and anti-inflammatories, should not be the first choice. “We need to look at therapies that are nonpharmacological first,” he said. “That is a change.”</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="363" data-total-count="1186">The recommendations come as the United States is struggling with an epidemic of opioid addiction that often begins with a simple prescription for ailments like back pain. In recent years, a number of states have enacted measures aimed at curbing prescription painkillers. The problem has also led many doctors around the country to reassess prescribing practices.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div id="story-continues-1" class="story-interrupter"></div>
<div class="story-body-supplemental">
<div class="story-body story-body-2">
<p id="story-continues-2" class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="74" data-total-count="1260">The group did not address surgery. Its focus was on noninvasive treatment.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="613" data-total-count="1873">The new guidelines said that doctors should avoid prescribing opioid painkillers for relief of back pain and suggested that before patients try anti-inflammatories or muscle relaxants, they should try alternative therapies like exercise, <a class="meta-classifier" title="Recent and archival health news about acupuncture." href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/news/health/diseasesconditionsandhealthtopics/acupuncture/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier">acupuncture</a>, massage therapy or yoga. Doctors should reassure their patients that they will get better no matter what treatment they try, the group said. The guidelines also said that <a class="meta-classifier" title="Recent and archival health news about steroids." href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/news/health/diseasesconditionsandhealthtopics/steroids/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier">steroid</a> injections were not helpful, and neither was acetaminophen, like Tylenol, although other over-the-counter pain relievers like aspirin, naproxen or ibuprofen could provide some relief.</p>
<p id="story-continues-3" class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="188" data-total-count="2061">Dr. Weinstein, who was not an author of the guidelines, said patients have to stay active and wait it out. “Back pain has a natural course that does not require intervention,” he said.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="331" data-total-count="2392">In fact, for most of the people with acute back pain — defined as present for four weeks or less that does not radiate down the leg — there is no need to see a doctor at all, said Dr. Rick Deyo, a spine researcher and professor at the Oregon Health and Science University in Portland, Ore., and an author of the new guidelines.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="206" data-total-count="2598">“For acute back pain, the analogy is to the <a class="meta-classifier" title="In-depth reference and news articles about Colds." href="http://health.nytimes.com/health/guides/disease/common-cold/overview.html?inline=nyt-classifier">common cold</a>,” Dr. Deyo said. “It is very common and very annoying when it happens. But most of the time it will not result in anything major or serious. ”</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="234" data-total-count="2832">Even those with chronic back pain — lasting at least 12 weeks — should start with nonpharmacological treatments, the guidelines say. If patients still want medication, they can try over-the-counter drugs like ibuprofen or aspirin.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="245" data-total-count="3077">Scans, like an <a class="meta-classifier" title="In-depth reference and news articles about MRI." href="http://health.nytimes.com/health/guides/test/mri/overview.html?inline=nyt-classifier">M.R.I.</a>, for diagnosis are worse than useless for back pain patients, members of the group said in telephone interviews. The results can be misleading, showing what look like abnormalities that actually are not related to the pain.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="223" data-total-count="3300">Measures that help patients get back to their usual routines can help along the way, as Sommer Kleweno Walley, 43, of Seattle, can attest. Last spring, she slipped on the stairs in her house and fell down hard, on her back.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="86" data-total-count="3386">“After a couple of hours I could barely walk,” she said. “I was in real pain.”</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="287" data-total-count="3673">She saw a physical therapist, but the pain persisted. Eleven days later, she showed up at the office of Dr. Christopher J. Standaert, a spine specialist at the University of Washington and Harborview Medical Center. She expected to receive an M.R.I., at least, and maybe a drug for pain.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="263" data-total-count="3936">But Dr. Standaert told her an M.R.I. would not make any difference in her diagnosis or recovery and that the main thing was to keep active. She ended up getting anti-inflammatory medication and doing <a class="meta-classifier" title="Recent and archival health news about physical therapy." href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/news/health/diseasesconditionsandhealthtopics/physicaltherapy/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier">physical therapy</a>. A few months later, her back stopped hurting.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="189" data-total-count="4125">It is surprising, some experts in back pain say, how often patients are helped by treatments that are not medical, even by a placebo that patients are told at the start is really a placebo.</p>
<p id="story-continues-4" class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="285" data-total-count="4410">Dr. Standaert cited a <a href="http://journals.lww.com/pain/Fulltext/2016/12000/Open_label_placebo_treatment_in_chronic_low_back.17.aspx">study</a> in which patients with chronic low back pain were offered a placebo, and were told it was a placebo, along with their usual treatment — often an anti-inflammatory drug like ibuprofen or naproxen. Or, the patients remained with their usual treatment alone.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="301" data-total-count="4711">Those taking the placebo reported less pain and disability than those in the control group who did not take it. The placebo effect, although modest, was about the same as the effect in studies testing nonpharmacological treatments for back pain like acupuncture, massage or chiropractic manipulations.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="300" data-total-count="5011">Many people with chronic back pain tend to shut down, avoiding their usual activities, afraid of making things worse, Dr. Standaert said. Helping them is not a matter of prescribing drugs but rather teaching them to set goals and work toward returning to an active life, even if they still have pain.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="132" data-total-count="5143">“They have to believe their life can get better,” Dr. Standaert said. “They have to believe they can get to a better state.”</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="52" data-total-count="5195">The question is: Will the new guidelines be adopted?</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="242" data-total-count="5437">“Patients are looking for a cure,” said Dr. Steven J. Atlas, a back pain specialist at Massachusetts General Hospital, who wrote an <a href="http://annals.org/aim/article/doi/10.7326/M17-0293">editorial</a> accompanying the article on the new recommendations. “The guidelines are for managing pain.”</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="302" data-total-count="5739">Added to the problem are the incentives that push doctors and patients toward medications, scans and injections, Dr. Deyo said. “There is marketing from professional organizations and from industry,” he said. “‘We have the cure. You can expect to be cured. You can expect to be pain free.’”</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="215" data-total-count="5954">Medical insurance also contributes to the treatment problem, back experts say, because it does not pay for remedies like mindfulness training or chiropractic manipulations which, Dr. Deyo added, “are not cheap.”</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="100" data-total-count="6054">Even if doctors want to recommend such treatments, there is no easy referral system, Dr. Atlas said.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="120" data-total-count="6174">“It is much easier at Mass General to get a shot than to get a mind-body or cognitive behavioral therapy,” he added.</p>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="289" data-total-count="6463">Dr. Weinstein has a prescription: “What we need to do is to stop medicalizing symptoms,” he said. Pills are not going to make people better and as for other treatments, he said, “yoga and tai chi, all those things are wonderful, but why not just go back to your normal activities?”</p>
<p id="story-continues-5" class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="78" data-total-count="6541" data-node-uid="1">“I know your back hurts, but go run, be active, instead of taking a pill.”</p>
