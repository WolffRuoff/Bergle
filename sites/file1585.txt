<h2>Look what you can do now…</h2>



<h3>Write now, send later with Schedule send in Gmail <br></h3>



<ul><li>When drafting an original email or reply in Gmail, you’ll now see an arrow next to the “Send” button.<ul><li>Clicking this arrow now gives you the option “Schedule send” option.</li><li>After clicking “Schedule send”, you can pick the specific date &amp; time you’d like the message to be delivered.</li></ul></li><li>Scheduled messages will appear in the “Scheduled” folder while queued to be sent.</li><li>Learn more:  <a href="https://support.google.com/mail/answer/9214606">https://support.google.com/mail/answer/9214606</a> </li></ul>



<h3>Save time with new scheduling features in Calendar </h3>



<ul><li>Peek at calendars and automatically add guests</li><li>More fields in the creation pop-up dialog</li><li>Learn more:  <a href="https://support.google.com/calendar/answer/72143">https://support.google.com/calendar/answer/72143</a> <a href="https://support.google.com/calendar/answer/72143"></a><br></li></ul>



<h3>Set start times and import reminders in Tasks </h3>



<ul><li>Set a date and time for your tasks and receive notifications</li><li>Create repeating tasks</li><li>Import reminders into Tasks</li><li>Learn more:  <a href="https://support.google.com/tasks/answer/7675838">https://support.google.com/tasks/answer/7675838</a> <br></li></ul>

<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="alignright is-resized"><img src="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/IMG_3302-2-1024x768.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-848" width="329" height="247" srcset="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/IMG_3302-2-1024x768.jpg 1024w, http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/IMG_3302-2-300x225.jpg 300w, http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/IMG_3302-2-768x576.jpg 768w, http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/IMG_3302-2-1200x900.jpg 1200w" sizes="(max-width: 329px) 100vw, 329px" /><figcaption>Scene from episode #1</figcaption></figure></div>



<p>We’ve hinted at it in the last two IT/DL Video Updates. Well here it is, folks! If you’re ready to learn, we’re ready to roll with … <em>Golf Cart Pedagogy.</em> <br></p>



<p>Golf cart what? Pedagogy who? <br></p>



<p>Allow us to explain. Golf Cart Pedagogy is yet another vehicle (see what we did there &#8211; vehicle?!) for faculty, staff and students to discuss the ways they’re using technology to support teaching and learning here at Muhlenberg. This video series will explore the creative ways our community members are using edtech of all shapes and sizes.<br></p>



<p>We hope these conversations (on four wheels) will inspire you to explore new tools, or use current tool in new ways. Plus we think it’s a pretty neat way to introduce the community to members that they may only see in passing on Academic Row.<br></p>



<p>We in IT/MS hope that you will find this series as exciting as we do! With that, we invite your feedback&#8230; <a href="mailto:itms@muhlenberg.edu">itms@muhlenberg.edu.</a> </p>



<p>If you would like to nominate yourself or a colleague for a future episode of Golf Cart Pedagogy, or you have a topic you’d like us to explore, please email us at <a href="mailto:itms@muhlenberg.edu">itms@muhlenberg.edu.</a><br><br></p>



<p>Without further ado&#8230;please join us on the inaugural voyage of Golf Cart Pedagogy!  <a href="https://video.muhlenberg.edu/media/Golf+Cart+Pedagogy+-+Episode++1+-+%22Here+Be+Dragons%22/1_gbx7oxtm/117597021">Click here to view!</a><br></p>

	<div class="entry-content">
		<p dir="ltr">October is <a href="https://staysafeonline.org/ncsam/themes/">National Cyber Security Awareness Month</a>. We have two significant efforts taking place. The first is the implementation of a new security training and awareness program that will highlight how to spot phishing emails, avoid viruses, and be more cyber aware in general. Phishing remains the number 1 entry point to account compromises and system breaches. Be on the lookout for a training email that will connect you to our KnowBe4 platform, eventually available via our Application Portal. This represents a big step forward for us in our Cyber Security efforts.</p>
<p dir="ltr">More importantly, we will be launching full desk encryption on Apple laptops. Note that there will be virtually no change to the Apple user experience &#8211; you still log on with a single password at a single point. No double entry or multiple passwords. It just happens a bit earlier in the startup process. This will eventually spread to Microsoft units as well, once we finish vetting software solutions and verifying how they will function.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Encryption is key to our overall cyber security position, and I described its benefits in more detail in <a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2018/06/11/it-protects-full-disk-encryption-and-protecting-against-data-breaches/">an earlier blog post</a>. A lost or stolen laptop without encryption constitutes a data breach. At the least this triggers our cyber security insurance and a long process of forensics and notifications. At the most it jeopardizes actual user data and puts personal information at risk.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Encryption, as the word suggests, “hides” the data on your hard disk behind a security key associated with your password. Only once you enter your valid password will the disk be decrypted and readable. Today, should your laptop be lost or stolen, a bad actor could simply plug that hard drive into another computer and read files and data. With encryption, that would not be possible.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Turning on encryption does not require any intervention on your part. We can do this via policies in our management software, JAMF. What&#8217;s great is that the system uses the native Apple FileVault service. We turn it on via JAMF, but it&#8217;s not actually using any proprietary configurations or settings. JAMF lets us insure that encryption is running.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Our tests thus far indicate that there is not a performance hit due to encryption, a common question that has arisen.</p>

	<div class="entry-content">
		<p>June 25th saw the launch of 2-Factor Authentication for off-campus access to our Application Portal, for staff only. Since it&#8217;s off campus-only and not everyone needs to get to the portal outside of work hours, we understand that not everyone would sign up. Still, over 500 users have done so, and we&#8217;re happy to report that the roll-out has gone smoothly, for the most part.</p>
<p>As a quick overview/reminder, 2-Factor Authentication means that you have to provide a 2nd &#8220;proof&#8221; of identity when logging in to our Application Portal. The 1st factor is your password, which can be stolen or hacked via a number of methods (see our passwords &amp; passphrases blog post for how easy it is to crack a password). The 2nd factor is much more controlled. Usually in the form of a code available on or a text sent to your phone, the premise is that only you have the device, so it&#8217;s safe to presume it&#8217;s really you when you&#8217;re logging in.</p>
<p>We offer 2nd factors in the form of an app on your smart phone, a text sent to any type of phone, or a physical, hardware &#8220;token.&#8221; The last option is just a little device you plug into the USB port on your computer that sends out a verification code.</p>
<p>So what&#8217;s next?</p>
<p>We&#8217;re weighing a lot of different options right now, all of which are based on industry standards, best practices, and what we&#8217;re seeing at other institutions. Security is a serious matter and it takes all of us doing our respective parts to ensure good cyber security hygiene. Some things we&#8217;re considering is expanding the group of users that must have a 2nd factor, making 2nd factor required from all locations, or staying the course. Different institutions have done different things, but 2 factor is becoming more and more of a reality across higher education.</p>

<article id="post-782" class="post post-782 type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-security tag-2fa tag-passphrase tag-passwords">
	<!-- Grab the featured image -->
	
	<header class="entry-header">
				<div class="entry-date">
			<span class="posted-on"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?p=782" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date published" datetime="2018-07-13T10:47:32-04:00">July 13, 2018</time></a></span><span class="byline"> by </span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?author=1499">Allan Chen</a></span>		</div><!-- .entry-meta -->
		
		<h1 class="entry-title"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?p=782" rel="bookmark">IT-Protects! &#8211; Strong Passwords (and Passphrases)</a></h1>	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	
<div class="entry-meta">
	
	<ul class="meta-list">
					<li class="meta-cat"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?cat=8070" rel="category">Security</a></li>
							<li class="meta-tag"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=2fa" rel="tag">2fa</a>, <a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=passphrase" rel="tag">passphrase</a>, <a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=passwords" rel="tag">passwords</a></li>
						<li class="meta-comment">
			<span class="comments-link"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?p=782#respond">Leave a comment</a></span>
		</li>
					</ul>
</div>
	<div class="entry-content">
		<p><span style="font-weight: 400">The definition of a strong password has changed over the last couple of years. Before, the recommendations included complexity (numbers and punctuation instead of letters) and frequent resets (in some cases as little as 30 days). The latest <a href="https://www.nist.gov/blogs/taking-measure/easy-ways-build-better-p5w0rd">NIST guidelines</a> have shifted, however, towards length and use of memorable but difficult-to-hack passphrases. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">In the past, complex passwords were recommended. Examples could include:</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">h0m38ddr3ss!</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">S8ndW1ch#</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">T3l3ph0n3Numb3r@</span></li>
</ul>
<p>The problem with complex passwords is that they are actually fairly predictable. They are generally based on a one word, or two associated words. The replacing of letters has a pattern. “1” became !, for instance, or an “a” became an “8.” And throwing punctuation at the end added only a small number of actual variations. When hackers are using brute force techniques (1000 guesses/second), any amount of predictability is dangerous. And password expiration is a problem because, simply put, it’s hard to remember passwords when they have to change even every 6 months, much less 30 days. We end up using simpler and simpler passwords, just adding a number at the end of them, or writing them down in order to remember them.</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">The new recommendations are for a longer password or, even better, a passphrase rather than a single word. Better yet, make it a combination of somewhat unrelated words that is memorable specifically to you. An example might be something as logical as “sunwalkraindrive” or something along those lines. No numbers, no upper and lower case, no punctuation. Incredibly hard to crack. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">A fabulous graphic borrowed from </span><a href="http://www.nist.gov"><span style="font-weight: 400">nist.gov</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> helps describe the reasons why a longer passphrase is so powerful. </span><a href="https://www.nist.gov/blogs/taking-measure/easy-ways-build-better-p5w0rd"><span style="font-weight: 400">The entire article (also linked above), in fact, is great</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400">. </span></p>
<p><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/password_strength.png"><img class="aligncenter wp-image-783 size-full" src="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/password_strength.png" alt="" width="740" height="601" srcset="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/password_strength.png 740w, http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/password_strength-300x244.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 740px) 100vw, 740px" /></a><br />
<span style="font-weight: 400">While we at Muhlenberg are not quite ready to switch fully to the NIST standards, we have already shifted to a 12 character password. We have a less frequent reset interval, and will explore changes as part of the shifting landscape of both cyber security in general (e.g. new NIST guidelines) and at Muhlenberg specifically (e.g. impact of two-factor authentication).</span></p>
			</div><!-- .entry-content -->

<div class="entry-meta">
	
	<ul class="meta-list">
					<li class="meta-cat"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?cat=8070" rel="category">Security</a></li>
							<li class="meta-tag"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=breaches" rel="tag">breaches</a>, <a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=encryption" rel="tag">encryption</a></li>

	<div class="entry-content">
		<p><span style="font-weight: 400">The latest IT Protects! monthly newsletter brings up the topic of disk encryption and data breaches. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">An unencrypted hard drive (the main storage device in your computer, desktop or laptop) can be easily accessed through other devices. Take the drive out, connect it to another computer, and all files are viewable and able to be copied. It’s disturbingly straightforward. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">An encrypted drive, however, requires a password to get to the data, even when plugged into another computer. To the user, this appears just as an extra login step, during computer start-up. But to would-be hackers, it’s a major obstacle.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">In fact, should a laptop be lost or stolen, for instance, an unencrypted drive would be considered a data breach in most instances. An encrypted drive, however, does not meet this qualification as our data is still safe and secure. This is a major difference to us from a security perspective, as well as from a policy one. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Starting around late fall (and ideally during Cyber Security Awareness Month in October), OIT will mandate full disk encryption on all college-owned laptops. At a later date, we will require it on desktops for frequent users of sensitive data. We will utilize existing tools to do encryption and passkey management (in case you forget your password). </span></p>

	<header class="entry-header">
				<div class="entry-date">
			<span class="posted-on"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?p=742" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date published" datetime="2018-05-03T10:53:06-04:00">May 3, 2018</time><time class="updated" datetime="2018-06-11T09:36:44-04:00">June 11, 2018</time></a></span><span class="byline"> by </span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?author=1499">Allan Chen</a></span>		</div><!-- .entry-meta -->
		
		<h1 class="entry-title"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?p=742" rel="bookmark">IT Protects! &#8211; Two Step Authentication and Safe Guarding Your Account</a></h1>	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	
<div class="entry-meta">
	
	<ul class="meta-list">
					<li class="meta-cat"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?cat=8070" rel="category">Security</a></li>
							<li class="meta-tag"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=2-factor" rel="tag">2-factor</a>, <a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=2fa" rel="tag">2fa</a>, <a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=mfa" rel="tag">mfa</a>, <a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=security" rel="tag">Security</a>, <a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?tag=two-step-authentication" rel="tag">two-step authentication</a></li>
						<li class="meta-comment">
			<span class="comments-link"><a href="http://it.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/?p=742#respond">Leave a comment</a></span>
		</li>
					</ul>
</div>
	<div class="entry-content">
		<p>In this issue of IT Protects!, we&#8217;ll be discussing Two-Factor Authentication. This is one of the most important security initiatives we have in motion here in OIT and at Muhlenberg.</p>
<p>Also known as Multi-factor, Two-Step, or Dual Factor Authentication, this technology requests a second form of verification when logging into an account. Already widely available on systems ranging from GMail to Amazon to Dropbox to financial institutions, it works fairly simply, but is incredibly powerful.</p>
<ol>
<li>You log into your account at a supported service with your username and password (which is your first &#8220;factor&#8221; or step)</li>
<li>You get a text message or some other alert on your phone or utilize a hardware token* to confirm that it was you that entered that password (this confirmation is the second factor or step). This is usually some kind of 6 digit code that you enter.</li>
</ol>
<p>*a hardware token is just any kind of physical item or device that will generate the second factor. This might be something you have on your key chain that generates 6 digit codes, or a USB device that you have to plug into your computer.</p>
<p>The key part is that the phone or hardware token is likely to be in your physical possession. Someone else might have your password (via a successful phishing scam, for instance), but they won&#8217;t likely have your phone, too, or that hardware token. Obviously, if you get an alert when you haven&#8217;t been trying to log in, go right away and change your password, too. But it&#8217;s good to know that an unauthorized user can&#8217;t get in without that second factor.</p>
<p>We will eventually be adding two-factor authentication to the Application Portal. This will immediately protect you for your GSuite (Gmail, Drive, etc), Workday activities, the Canvas LMS and a number of other services all in one fell swoop.</p>
<p>This certainly introduces some complexities and challenges. You always have to have your phone on you, or that hardware token handy. It&#8217;ll take (seconds) longer to get into your accounts, and with our decision to two-step authenticate our Application Portal, that means a great many applications will require a second step. But the security benefits far outweigh the inconveniences, in our opinion (and the opinion of many other institutions and security groups).</p>
			</div><!-- .entry-content -->
