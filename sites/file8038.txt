          <h1 class="entry-title ">Why Major In Dance?</h1><hr class="featurette-divider __before_content">        </header>
        
        <div class="entry-content">
          <p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">Sage Advise from Marilyn Vos Savant</span></strong></p>
<p><strong>Should young people be allowed to study what they love rather than </strong></p>
<p><strong>what they need to know?</strong></p>
<p><strong></strong></p>
<p>Ask Marilyn: I am dissatisfied with my high school schedule. I enjoy the arts much more than the sciences, and Ive completed my required science classes. Im enrolled in chemistry only because Im ranked No. 7 in my class of 130, so the system thinks I should be in science classes instead of the arts classes I elected. I find chemistry hard, but singing and writing make me happy, and Id like to pursue a career in music education or write a novel.</p>
<p>Do you think it is more important for a person to study subjects solely for the purpose of advancing his or her intelligence, or should one take subjects that one enjoys and might use in the future?</p>
<p>Amanda Leigh, Idabel Okla.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>I believe that the younger the child, the more important it is to study subjects that will develop his or her individual potential to function intellectually. Any normal or bright youngster in elementary school must learn to read, write and do math at increasingly difficult levels. With time, this expands to include reading, literature, writing essays and handling the math and logic of the sciences, including the science of mathematics. One cannot allow a third-grader to finger paint his life away.</p>
<p>That said, I also believe that the older the person, the less important it is to study these subjects and the more important it is to study the subjects he or she loves. Competition in the real world is fierce, and success is far more often achieved by those who love what they do, than by those who dont. Even with your intelligence, you probably wouldnt be a successful chemist. An equally bright chemist who loves his or her work will easily do better. You have more chance for success in a music or writing career. Even if you never win an award, and the world never knows your name, you still will have the sweetest consolation prize of all: <strong>You will be happy!</strong></p>
<p><strong></strong></p>
<p><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">The Value of an Arts Education</span></strong></p>
<p><strong>Arts education contributes to business career success</strong></p>
<p><strong></strong></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li>More and more research is becoming available that shows that arts integration programs and the study of the arts in schools develops characteristics in students that may be applied to all forms of future endeavor, including careers in our local business community that seem unrelated to the arts.</li>
<li>The study of arts also encourages a suppleness of mind, tolerance for ambiguity, and the ability to make trade-offs among alternative courses of action, says Nancy Matheny. Business prefers those who can shift gears quickly over those who have rigid thinking. Studying the arts helps students to think and work across traditional disciplines. The ability to think outside ones discipline and understand how one part fits into the whole is highly valued by business todayand it is one of the things that leads to innovation.</li>
<li>Adaptability to change may be one of the most crucial and desirable characteristics of an employee in todays work force.</li>
<li>The characteristic of artists that make them uniquely useful in various business functions would be imagination and creativity, the ability to abstract concepts from one context and reapply them in a totally different context, sensitivity to the human condition, the ability to perceive patterns and trends, the ability to integrate, and an inclination to experiment. People with all of these characteristics tend to be intelligent people.</li>
<li>An education in the arts teaches students how to work cooperatively and how to work out conflicting points of view. Performing in a dance group is a cooperative activity that fosters team workanother characteristic that business values.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong></strong></p>
<p>(Information from artLINKS June/July 2000)</p>

<p><strong><br />
</strong><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">Dance Education: Can I Make a Living?</span></strong></p>
<p>By: Stephanie Spencer</p>
<p>I have spent the last ten years of my life trying to convince myself, and those around me, that dance education is a viable career choice. Dance education is based on the comparison of how your body moves and feels in space and how others move and feel in space. Through dance education you can broaden your sense of self and awareness of others. It is the science of nonverbal communication; a language that is often overlooked or remains subconscious in our society. This language is a powerful tool in relating to yourself and the world around you. So, why did it take me so many years to convince myself that I could have a viable career teaching dance?</p>
<p>I started as a pre-medical student as an undergraduate because it seemed like an esteemed career choice. It did not occur to me that I could make a living with dance. I was told to make it my avocation, not my vocation. I remember going to a guidance counselor that first week of college and looking through all the majors offered. There was a dance major, but I felt that my career options in dance were less stable than pre-med.</p>
<p>Two years later, I really wasnt happy with my choice to follow pre-med. From dawn to dusk, I was stuck inside a concrete lab staring into a microscope, studying things that didnt have much meaning to me. I wanted to learn about the people around me, not things I could barely see. I felt empty. Luckily, in order to fulfill my pre-med requirements, I took a literature class, and to my surprise I loved it! I was reading about exciting characters in various places and learning about how people think and live. At the end of term, the professor had a party for us all and asked each of us our major. When I told her I was pre-med, she laughed. She actually thought I was joking. She said that I didnt strike her as the pre-med type and that I should come see her when I decided to switch majors.</p>
<p>Soon after, I made another visit to a guidance counselor and he gave me some great advice. He said that I needed to <strong>decide on my career by choosing something I like to do as much as eating ice cream</strong>. I wondered if I could choose my favorite subject ad still make a living, as I was stuck on the idea that college was about choosing a major that would lead to a definite job. He was trying to tell me that your passion could be your work. And a passionate and fulfilling life is definitely better than eating ice cream.</p>
<p>Eventually, I realized that no one was going to recommend dance as a career for me; if I wanted to dance, I was going to have to make it happen myself. I went to see my professors in the dance department and told them I wanted to go to graduate school in dance. I wanted to apply to the most incredible dance programs in the world. I purposely picked high-ranking graduate schools in order to test my abilities. If I got into tone of these schools it would confirm my gut feeling that I was talented enough to make it in the dance world. If I didnt get into any of these schools, then I would choose another path.</p>
<p>Believe it or not, I was accepted to all the schools. I was shocked. My parents were even more shocked. I choose to go to the Laban Centre in London, where I was simultaneously enrolled in a professional dance diploma and masters program. While enjoying my studies in London, I was advised that an M.F.A. degree from the United States has the greatest weight when applying for full-time teaching position at a college. So I came back to the U.S. and finished my M.F.A. degree at the California Institute of the Arts.</p>
<p>With my terminal degree in hand, I was still scared to declare dance as my livelihood. I sent out my resume to every community college in California, and worried that there might not be a job out there for me. I called up fellow alumni, who told me not to give up. I went to bookstores to research other career choices. Maybe I would make a good physical therapist? Yet, by mid-summer, I had job offers from two different colleges and was working with the California Arts Project.</p>
<p>Finally, one day while teaching movement to a group of non-dancers at a California Arts Project workshop, I finally embraced the reality that dance is a valuable and viable career choice. One of the students told me that he really appreciated my class because the idea of movement opened up a new way of seeing and appreciating himself and others around him. His words felt so good to me. I felt validated that I persevered through my doubts to find a career that expanded someones view of the world. I had made the right career choice.</p>
<p>After all my years of worrying, I have never had trouble finding a job in the dance world. Sometimes you have to piece a living together, but it is always more interesting and rewarding. I really love what I do, more than eating ice cream. <strong>Dance is necessary in our culture to teach people about their bodies, about the joy of movement and all movement means in the context of society and interpersonal relationships. Even if you do not find a job teaching dance, the education you will receive during the process of attaining a dance degree will be valuable in anything you do. Dance education teaches you about yourself, how you relate to others and how others relate to you. These are priceless skills in any career and in life.</strong></p>
<p>(Dance Magazine College Guide, Page 118)</p>

<p>As a dancer first entering college, I thought I was well trained and knowledgeable about dance. Then, I found out there was so much more in the dance field than fine-tuning the movement of the body. Dancing also fine-tunes the mind and imagination in ways that few other majors in college do.</p>
<p>Jennifer Volk 00</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li>When I came into Muhlenberg, I thought that by the time I graduated Id be able to do five pirouettes, 32 fouettes on pointe, and have extension to my ear. After my first few ballet classes, I realized that I had many more important things to be working on.</li>
<li>As a result of the core courses in the major, you have a new understanding and appreciation for everything being taught to you in technique classes.</li>

<li>It has opened me up, I am more willing to try something new and accept something different.</li>

<p>Tanya Nicklous 00</p>

<ul>
<li>Ive learned to question the information given to me, to always ask WHY? Ive learned that the accepted way isnt always the best way.</li>
<li>Critical thinking, organizing ideas, problem solving, leadership skills, communication, abstract thinking, and being able to give and receive feedback are a few skills learned while being a dance major.</li>
<li>I understand <em>my</em> anatomic structure and how to use it to <em>my</em> I know how to take information given to me by a teacher and to adapt it safely and most efficiently to <em>my</em> body.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>A person can take dance technique class after dance technique class, and learn a lot about dance. But do they really understand what they are doing and why? Can they appreciate historical references when seeing works? Can they appreciate the process of choreography, its challenges and choices?</p>
<p>Beth Danton 00</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li>Learning about choreography and teaching opens you up to other job opportunities that I never thought I had.</li>
<li>Ive noticed a huge difference in my personality since I first came here, and I attribute that to my dance courses. Ive become much more outgoing, and for the first time, I feel like Im able to speak in front of large groups of people. I feel that I can speak intelligently in group discussions and debates. I learned how to be a confident, informed speaker as a dance major.</li>
<li>To be able to spend time really learning about something youve always loved is an amazing opportunity, especially in the college environment.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>People ask what Im going to do with a major in dance. Apparently they think that they only thing I can do is either teach or perform. Im not worried because I feel prepared for anything. If I need to know more about a specific area, I know how to learn. Everyone in college chooses a specific major. Some majors teach you everything you need to know in that specific area, like how to get into medical school, or how to be a teacher. Other majors teach you all of the skills you need in that area, plus everything you need to know to excell at life. The dance major falls in the latter category. Everyone studies life, but we just choose which medium to use to get that information. Some people learn about life through student teaching, others through biology, or theatre, or literature. I learned life through dance! J</p>
<p>Christina Spring 03</p>
<p>Always be thankful for the ability to move!</p>
