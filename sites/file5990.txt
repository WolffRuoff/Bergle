	<article id="post-240" class="post-240 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-baseball category-womens-basketball">
		<header class="entry-header">
						<h1 class="entry-title"><a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2011/10/04/saac-attack/" rel="bookmark">SAAC Attack</a></h1>
			
						<div class="entry-meta">
				<span class="sep">Posted on </span><a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2011/10/04/saac-attack/" title="3:37 pm" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date" datetime="2011-10-04T15:37:58-04:00">October 4, 2011</time></a><span class="by-author"> <span class="sep"> by </span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/author/sportsinfo/" title="View all posts by sportsinfo" rel="author">sportsinfo</a></span></span>			</div><!-- .entry-meta -->
			
					</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p><em>The Student-Athlete Advisory Committee (SAAC) was adopted by the NCAA in 1989, formed primarily to review and offer student-athlete input on NCAA activities and proposed legislation that affect student-athlete welfare. NCAA legislation mandates that all member institutions have SAACs on their respective campuses. Last weekend, junior Kate Clawson (basketball) and senior Nick Busillo (baseball) represented Muhlenberg at the Centennial Conference SAAC Leadership Retreat. Here&#8217;s what they wrote about their experience:</em></p>
<p><a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/clawsonhs.jpg"><img class="alignleft size-full wp-image-241" src="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/clawsonhs.jpg" alt="" width="100" height="150" /></a>(KC) The SAAC retreat was held at the Wyndham Hotel in Harrisburg, Pa., and was attended by 20 student-athletes, about two from each school. On Friday, when we arrived we met Keith Waldman, from OPA, who does team building with teams all over the nation. First, he led us through some team building activities to better get to know each other. All the athletes were very welcoming, as was Renee Fraker, the director of championships in our conference who put the retreat together.</p>
<p>(NB) There were two baseball players from Ursinus at the conference. It was funny because of course my natural instinct was to not like these kids, but they were both nice kids. Jokes and predictions were made, and that made things interesting, but all in all they were great kids and it was a great experience.</p>
<p>(KC) I really enjoyed the team building activities because each of them taught us things we <a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_clawson.jpg"><img class="alignright size-full wp-image-245" src="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_clawson.jpg" alt="" width="400" height="306" srcset="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_clawson.jpg 400w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_clawson-300x229.jpg 300w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_clawson-392x300.jpg 392w" sizes="(max-width: 400px) 100vw, 400px" /></a>could apply to accomplishing team goals, staying positive, learning from our mistakes and becoming better leaders. This was definitely one of my favorite parts of the trip. We also brought along our resumes for the professional development portion of the weekend where we talked about the importance of networking and interview tips, which were very beneficial for everyone.</p>
<p>On Saturday we talked about issues that athletes face on campus and problem solving for each of the issues. Everyone shared their ideas, which were very helpful, and it was cool to see what each school had to say.</p>
<p><em></em><a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/busillohs.jpg"><img class="alignleft size-full wp-image-242" src="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/busillohs.jpg" alt="" width="100" height="150" /></a>(NB) One common issue that I noticed was the balancing of sports and academics. Although presently we are not fans of the workload, we all recognized that in the long run this will all be beneficial for us. We all recognized that we are students first and that it comes with being a student-athlete.</p>
<p>(KC) Next we went over the proposals for new NCAA legislation. This was really important because it gave us as student-athletes a say in what regulations were being passed. We got to offer our opinions and knew that they were actually going to matter.</p>
<p>At the end of a long day on Saturday we met with members from the Special Olympics, had a pizza party and played bocce with them. We definitely had a thing or two to learn about bocce as we saw that the Special Olympics athletes weren&#8217;t taking it easy on us. I think we all got beat that day, but it was still really fun to hang out with them and learn from them. I think it taught us how determined the athletes of Special Olympics are, and it was <a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_busillo.jpg"><img class="alignright size-full wp-image-246" src="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_busillo.jpg" alt="" width="450" height="301" srcset="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_busillo.jpg 450w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_busillo-300x200.jpg 300w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/10/saac_busillo-448x300.jpg 448w" sizes="(max-width: 450px) 100vw, 450px" /></a>pretty inspirational to hear some of their stories, one woman who got to go to China for the Olympics and had been a member since the 1970s.</p>
<p>Overall, I had a great weekend, met really influential people, and learned a lot that I can take back to the SAAC group as well as to Muhlenberg athletics as a whole.</p>
<p>(NB) During discussions I learned of all the fundraising activities that other schools are doing. I know Muhlenberg does their part in fundraising, but these schools had some great ideas that would not be bad thing for Muhlenberg to take a look into.</p>

						<h1 class="entry-title"><a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2011/09/28/money-mule-ball/" rel="bookmark">Money-Mule-Ball</a></h1>
			
						<div class="entry-meta">
				<span class="sep">Posted on </span><a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2011/09/28/money-mule-ball/" title="3:02 pm" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date" datetime="2011-09-28T15:02:26-04:00">September 28, 2011</time></a><span class="by-author"> <span class="sep"> by </span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/author/sportsinfo/" title="View all posts by sportsinfo" rel="author">sportsinfo</a></span></span>			</div><!-- .entry-meta -->
			
					</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p>One of the most popular movies in the United States has a Muhlenberg connection.</p>
<p><em>Moneyball</em>, starring Brad Pitt, is the story of Billy Beane&#8217;s doings as general manager of <a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/215px-Moneyball_Poster.jpg"><img class="alignright size-full wp-image-212" src="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/215px-Moneyball_Poster.jpg" alt="" width="215" height="319" srcset="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/215px-Moneyball_Poster.jpg 215w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/215px-Moneyball_Poster-202x300.jpg 202w" sizes="(max-width: 215px) 100vw, 215px" /></a>the Oakland A&#8217;s in 2002. It opened last weekend to critical acclaim and finished at No. 2 at the box office, behind only <em>The Lion King 3D</em>.</p>
<p>Former Mule baseball coach Bob Macaluso has a small part in the movie hitting fungoes to Scott Hatteberg (played by Chris Pratt).</p>
<p>&#8220;A friend of mine was hired by Sony Pictures to gather former players and coaches to try out for parts in the movie in the summer of 2010,&#8221; Macaluso wrote in an e-mail. &#8220;Most of the movie was shot in Oakland; my scene was at Long Beach. They selected a scene of me hitting ground balls to Hatteberg and got a couple of closeup shots of me on the big screen.&#8221; </p>
<p>Macaluso, billed as &#8220;Coach Bob&#8221; in the movie credits, coached Muhlenberg from 1999 to <a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/macaluso4.jpg"><img class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-213" src="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/macaluso4-156x300.jpg" alt="" width="156" height="300" srcset="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/macaluso4-156x300.jpg 156w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/macaluso4.jpg 248w" sizes="(max-width: 156px) 100vw, 156px" /></a>2006 and ranks second on the program&#8217;s all-time wins list with a record of 136-146-3. He led the Mules to the Centennial Conference finals in 2002 and 2003.</p>
<p>When he&#8217;s not working on his movie career, Macaluso is in his fifth year as an assistant coach at Cal-Irvine. He coaches first base for the Anteaters, who have been to the NCAA Regionals each of the last four years.</p>
<p>So how has Macaluso&#8217;s big screen debut been received? &#8220;I have received positive reviews of my Academy Award performance!&#8221; he wrote.</p>

	<article id="post-37" class="post-37 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-baseball">
		<header class="entry-header">
						<h1 class="entry-title"><a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2011/09/01/brett-rosen-international-man-of-baseball/" rel="bookmark">Brett Rosen, International Man of Baseball</a></h1>
			
						<div class="entry-meta">
				<span class="sep">Posted on </span><a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2011/09/01/brett-rosen-international-man-of-baseball/" title="8:51 am" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date" datetime="2011-09-01T08:51:07-04:00">September 1, 2011</time></a><span class="by-author"> <span class="sep"> by </span> <span class="author vcard"><a class="url fn n" href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/author/sportsinfo/" title="View all posts by sportsinfo" rel="author">sportsinfo</a></span></span>			</div><!-- .entry-meta -->
			
					</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p><em>On June 30, we wrote about baseball player Brett Rosen, who had just completed his freshman year at Muhlenberg, <a title="Mule Sports Daily - June 30, 2011" href="http://www.muhlenberg.edu/main/athletics/spring11/0630.html" target="_blank">qualifying to play for the British national team</a>. Here&#8217;s a follow-up on how he did:</em></p>
<p>After playing hundreds of games during his baseball career, Brett Rosen has grown accustomed to the normal routine of standing along the base line for the playing of the “Star-Spangled Banner” before first pitch. </p>
<p>So when that routine was thrown for a loop before one of his games this past summer, it took his mind a couple moments to remember just what jersey he was wearing.</p>
<p>Rosen spent part of the summer playing for Team Great Britain in the European Championship qualifying tournament in Tel Aviv, Israel. And according to Muhlenberg sophomore, it wasn’t until he heard something other than that familiar tune that he truly<a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4608.jpg"><img class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-38" src="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4608-300x199.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="199" srcset="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4608-300x199.jpg 300w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4608-450x300.jpg 450w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4608.jpg 1000w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a> felt the magnitude of the moment.</p>
<p>“The overall experience of playing in an international competition was mind-blowing,” the catcher said. “It was surreal suiting up for, and representing, Great Britain. Honestly, it didn&#8217;t really hit me until they played ‘God Save the Queen’ before our first game. That was an incredible feeling.”</p>
<p>While he was born in the United States, Rosen’s mother was born in England, giving him dual citizenship. His brother, AJ, has competed for team Great Britain at the past two Winter Olympics.</p>
<p>Brett Rosen saw action in two games, both wins, to help Great Britain win the qualifier with a 4-1 record and advance to next summer’s European Championships in the Netherlands.</p>
<p>Rosen’s best game was against Georgia when he went 2-for-4 at the plate with a double and a stolen base in an 8-7 win. He also caught all nine innings in an 8-6 win over Israel <a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4614.jpg"><img class="alignright size-medium wp-image-39" src="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4614-300x199.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="199" srcset="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4614-300x199.jpg 300w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4614-450x300.jpg 450w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/IMG_4614.jpg 1000w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a>and finished the tournament with a .286 batting average.</p>
<p>The trip was also Rosen’s first time traveling the world alone, so when he wasn’t playing he used the opportunity to take in a lot of Israel’s ancient history.</p>
<p>“Tel Aviv was beautiful,” he said. “But Jerusalem was truly amazing. It was the best part of the trip. Our entire team was led all over the city on a tour. We got to see all the holy sites and the history behind them. That was very fascinating.”</p>
<p>One of the youngest members on the squad, Rosen said it did not take long to grow accustomed to his new teammates and international competition.</p>
<p>The team was comprised mostly of Canadians, Australians and Americans with British heritage. But while the players’ backgrounds may have differed drastically, Rosen said they all shared one common passion that brought them all together: baseball.</p>
<p><a href="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/DSC_2800.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-40" src="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/DSC_2800.jpg" alt="" width="1000" height="407" srcset="http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/DSC_2800.jpg 1000w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/DSC_2800-300x122.jpg 300w, http://sportsinfo.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/09/DSC_2800-500x203.jpg 500w" sizes="(max-width: 1000px) 100vw, 1000px" /></a></p>
<p>“It was great bonding with everyone, especially since so many of my teammates are from all over the world,” Rosen said. “One was from Calgary and another was from Australia; it was great getting to talk about our cultures.</p>
<p>“It also helped on the field because many of my teammates have been on the team for awhile and play professionally in leagues all over the world. I was able to learn a lot from their veteran knowledge.”</p>
<p>Rosen said the style of play did not differ much from the one he plays at Muhlenberg. But one big difference he did note was the passion of the fans.</p>
<p>While American baseball fans tend to be more laid back from pitch-to-pitch, Rosen said the crowds in Tel Aviv were loud and rowdy the entire game, especially during Great Britain’s three games with the host Israeli team, including the final.</p>
<p>But that just made winning the qualifier even more special, he said, adding he hopes his team can achieve similar success at next summer’s championship tournament.</p>
<p>“The atmosphere was definitely much more hostile,” he said. “I think this definitely made me a better player and should help during this college season.</p>
<p>“I can’t wait to go to Holland next summer to try to win the European Championship.”</p>
