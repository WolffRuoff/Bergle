	<div class="entry-content">
					<p>In the article “Want to be a better writer? Read more” by Mike Hanski, Hanski used this article to describe the four parts. The first part describes the importance of reading and a lot of famous writers use their words to support this view, for instance, Stephen said &#8220;“If you don’t have time to read, you don’t have the time (or the tools) to write. Simple as that&#8221; (Hanski 1). The second part is about the difference between a person who reads books and one that does not. In the third part Hanski states that reading can help students get smart and clear their brain when they are writing or studying in the school. The last part of this article, shows us the benefits of reading that people obtain in their life, and to prove that reading is good for people. “Write is a skill. But this skill is very complicated, because it can’t be implanted by simple learning through traditional grammar rules, punctuation marks, and different writing techniques.” (Hanski 1)I agree with that point, because all of the famous writer need to read so many books for gain new knowledge and write article again and again, perhaps they will failed once, twice or more. Finally they will succeed. Therefore, we know a lot of good and positive point right now.</p>
<p>But from my personal perspective, reading may also have a lot of negative side for the people. A good writer will give reader a feeling when they are reading, however the writer can also invoke a feeling of sadness, happiness or even fear. People should read a lot of books for themselves that is true, but not all books are to be read, some of the books you read may invoke a bad mood. These bad emotions are not as overcoming stress as Hanski said, (Hanski 2) maybe someone’s feelings are too depressed and then die after read a book. That is quite possible. Thereby, I think the author described in this article that it has an overwhelmingly positive outcome.</p>
<p>Hanski, M. (2014, April 22). Want to Be a Better Writer? Read More. The Huffington Post. Retrieved from http://www.huffingtonpost.com/mike-hanski/read-more_b_5192754.html</p>

One overlying theme that can be drawn from this article is that reading is a way of learning. The more you read, the more you learn new vocab, better your grammar, and learn ways to improve your writing skills.  These skills acquired from reading ultimately make you a better writer. Reading also helps you learn about the world around you. It connects you to the rest of the world, a source of endless knowledge that allows one to learn about all the current events happening around them. From reading you can find inspiration, what topics you may be more passionate about than others and digging deeper into that subject. The more you read, the more you become informed on certain topics and that helps someone start to generate some of their own ideas/opinions on a wide variety of things. Not only is reading a leisure activity to many, but more importantly it is a gateway to all kinds of learning.</p>

<article id="post-3376" class="post-3376 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-uncategorized">

	<div class="entry-details">
				<p>September 5, 2016<br/>
		by Justin Dalena<br/>
		<a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/09/05/read-to-write-2/#respond">0 comments</a></p>
	</div><!-- end entry-details -->
    
	<header class="entry-header">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/09/05/read-to-write-2/" title="Permalink to Read to write" rel="bookmark">Read to write</a></h2>
	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p>I don’t think anyone can dispute that being an avid reader opens your mind to appreciable amounts of possibilities that ultimately lead to greater success on multiple levels—socially, financially, interpersonally, athletically and intellectually&#8211; but does that genuinely mean that only those who are avid readers can become accomplished writers? What about the student who suffers from an auditory processing issue or some other type of learning disability that makes reading difficult and, in turn, writing a tedious chore? Or those who have to have to rely on books on tape to get through as assigned reading topic in order to understand what they have read. Would it be fair to say they can’t have as much success as a writer because they don’t actually read the words, but rather hear them. I would think not, but I am sure it is not with the same level of success. So for those who struggle with reading it would seem to me they are at a disadvantage when it comes to writing actually defining who they are. Instead, I struggle to understand why these individuals can’t reap all the other benefits being a reader provides—bigger vocabulary, greater knowledge, inspiration, motivation, effective communication skills—while not necessarily being the best writer. Or, alternatively, why can’t just a middle of the road reader become a great writer if writing is really about expressing of a person thinks.</p>

	<div class="entry-details">
				<p>September 4, 2016<br/>
		by Jamie Andrich<br/>
		<a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/09/04/want-to-be-a-better-writer-read-more-2/#respond">0 comments</a></p>
	</div><!-- end entry-details -->
    
	<header class="entry-header">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/09/04/want-to-be-a-better-writer-read-more-2/" title="Permalink to Want to Be a Better Writer? Read More." rel="bookmark">Want to Be a Better Writer? Read More.</a></h2>
	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p>&nbsp;<br />
Something as simple as reading can be an extremely useful tool in the pursuit to becoming an effective writer. Even though some may not recognize it, reading and writing go hand in hand. Reading, without a doubt, helps the writer process certain material that can certainly enhance one’s ability to convey what they have read into an effective writing piece. In the article, &#8220;Want to Be a Better Writer? Read More.&#8221;, Mark Hanski discusses the fact that reading is a very effective way to become a successful writer. So, to reinforce this thought, he brings up the fact that it is indeed scientifically proven that the more one reads the better they write. In an effort to explain this to the reader he states, &#8221; Science confirms that the reading has a positive influence on your brain: blood flows to it, improving brain&#8217;s functioning and connectivity&#8221;(Hanski,2). So therefore, reading enhances the brain which can lead to a positive outcome for the writer.</p>
<p>So, why not read more if it has no negative effect? Consequently, this statement may not be entirely true. The author fails to mention that undesirable and hostile readings can bring someone down emotionally. For example, negative comments and/or hate mail can negatively impact someone adversely. But, on the flip side, the author did mention that reading enjoyable books and other favorites can certainly brighten one’s mood. Therefore, if someone finds reading enjoyable why not read because it will could only result in an improved and positive mood and outcome. Who does not want to better themselves? Mark Hanski unquestionably and accurately emphasizes his point as to why people should read. After all, reading is a simple way to become a better writer and it can be useful in other ways at the same time. All readings portray some kind of emotion and expression, and when recognized can turn an average writer to a great writer.</p>
<p>Hanski, M. (2014, April 22). Want to Be A Better Writer? Read More. Huffington Post. Information from http://www.huffingtonpost.com/mike-hanski/read-more_b_5192754.htm</p>

	<header class="entry-header">
			<h2 class="entry-title"><a href="http://fys142f16.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2016/09/04/read-its-the-write-thing-to-do/" title="Permalink to Read. It&#8217;s the Write Thing to Do" rel="bookmark">Read. It&#8217;s the Write Thing to Do</a></h2>
	</header><!-- end entry-header -->
        
	<div class="entry-content">
					<p><em>Why do you read? Is it out of enjoyment? Obligation? Force?</em> As a kid, my mom would always force me to read because I felt that entertainment came out of objects with screens, not pieces of paper with ink. Little did I know that my writing skills would be affected because of my stubbornness towards reading. According to Mike Hanski in &#8220;Want to Be A Better Writer? Read More.&#8221;, &#8220;no good writing is possible without reading&#8221; (Hanski, 1). In this article, Hanski emphasizes the importance of the connection between writing and reading by repeating words like &#8220;helps&#8221; when listing the positive effects of practicing both skills.</p>
<p><em>So what? Why is it so important to be able to execute both skills? </em>It turns out that these simple life skills have a tremendous impact on our daily lives. For example, &#8220;according to the Basic Skills Agency&#8217;s study, pupils who are functionally illiterate at 16 consider school a simple waste of time and become most likely unemployed by the age of 30&#8221; (Hanski, 2). Even if a job did not require a whole lot of reading or writing (i.e. a carpenter, artist, construction worker), if you don&#8217;t know how to fill out a job application, then you don&#8217;t have a lot of options. Unemployment is a constant struggle for millions of Americans, and many probably don&#8217;t realize that learning these two common life skills can save them from financial struggles.</p>
<p>Moral of the story: make friends with these skills. They are more important than you think.</p>
