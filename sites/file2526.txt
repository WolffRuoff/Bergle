Retrieved April 25, 2011 from <a href="http://0-web.ebscohost.com.library.muhlenberg.edu/ehost/detail?vid=3&amp;hid=113&amp;sid=0b516150-c5ae-49a4-bc98-d9b18ce60e82%40sessionmgr114&amp;bdata=JnNpdGU9ZWhvc3QtbGl2ZSZzY29wZT1zaXRl#db=bsh&amp;AN=59915490">detail?vid=3&amp;hid=113&amp;sid=0b516150-c5ae-	49a4-bc98-	d9b18ce60e82%40sessionmgr114&amp;bdata=JnNpdGU9ZWhvc3QtbGl2ZSZzY29wZT1zaXRl#db=bsh &amp;AN=59915490</a></p>
<p><a href="http://mediaindustries11.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/04/turner_logo1.jpg"><img class="alignleft size-full wp-image-1159" src="http://mediaindustries11.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/files/2011/04/turner_logo1.jpg" alt="" width="132" height="99" /></a>As we have seen again and again, television companies and advertisers alike are increasingly worried that audiences just aren’t watching commercials anymore.  And why should they when the DVR makes it so easy to avoid them?  But the Turner Company, a cable unit owned by the media conglomerate Time Warner, is developing a new strategy to save commercials from falling to their death (ouch!).  The company has undergone a four year effort to create a new kind of advertising strategy that will offer advertisers on the cable networks TBX, TNT, and True something that many others do not: a strong link between the commercial and the TV show that the consumers are viewing. The goal of this effort is to bring consumers back to commercials by making these ads relevant to the specific program and the specific audience.</p>
<p>Turner’s idea is similar to a technique that other networks have come up with, the technique of embedding show content within the commercial breaks.  We saw American Idol trying out this strategy with their “embedded auditions” that ran between commercials.  Networks and advertisers hoped that when viewers saw a flash of that familiar American Idol logo, they would stop fast-forwarding to see the show content, and, with any luck, see some other ads in the process.<a class="more-link button" href="http://mediaindustries11.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2011/04/28/the-new-face-of-commercials/">Read More</a></p>

<p>Remember getting up early on Saturda<img class="alignleft" src="http://www.flowersbyvalerie.com/images/FBVED007.jpg" alt="" width="144" height="122" />y mornings to watch cartoons?  You undoubtedly remember such classics as <em>Recess </em>and <em>Pepper Ann</em>, but you probably also remember those bright, flashy advertisements for products like Gushers, Dunkaroos, and Kraft Mac and Cheese.  However, there have been some changes regarding food and drink advertising aimed towards kids.  After years of scrutiny from the FCC, 17 advertisers, including Burger King and Kraft, have not only improved the health content of their products, but have also changed their marketing strategies.  This agreement is called the Children’s Food and Beverage Advertising Initiative and is a creation of the Council of Better Business Bureaus.  In 2007, these advertisers pledged to promote healthy products in at least half of their media targeted to children.  In January 2010, promoting healthy products during kids’ ad times became an official requirement for businesses that are part of the Council of Better Business Bureaus.  Furthermore, major junk-food companies like Coca-Cola and Hershey have made it their policy to not advertise to children under the age of twelve.</p>
