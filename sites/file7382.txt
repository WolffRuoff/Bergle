		<p>Three key points that I found in this article are:<br />
-&#8220;Increasingly, we work for free for major Internet technology companies, on their platforms. We post our photos, our status updates, our articles, our discussions. We share, we like, and we retweet. Our content and our data, shared publicly, become theirs to profit from&#8221;. This point means something to me because when I do pretty much anything on social media, I don&#8217;t think about how I am profiting the website/app. I&#8217;ve always thought that the purpose of social media was to benefit the users&#8230;little did I know that the technology companies are manipulating the users to gain popularity.<br />
-“A woman must have money and a room of her own if she is to write fiction.” We can critique – and certainly we should – the class implications and expectations in Woolf’s commandment here; and we must consider both the financial burden and the transaction mechanism of a push for domains in education&#8221;. This point means something to me because I do agree with the article that this statement is wrong in that it implies that in order for a woman to be a successful writer, she needs to have wealth. This shuts down those who seek to be successful writers but don&#8217;t have the financial backup. It shouldn&#8217;t be the case that money is required to do a task as simple as writing.<br />
-&#8220;You do not own your Amazon Kindle books; you’ve purchased a license to access the content. Your heirs will not inherit your digital reading library. You do not own the music you stream; you’ve paid for a subscription. Your heirs will not inherit your digital music library. You don’t own the movies you watch via Netflix; again, it’s a subscription and unlike a print magazine subscription, once you stop paying the bill, you won’t have stacks of old copies lying about.&#8221; This point means something to me because it shows how technology companies trick us into thinking that by purchasing their products, we feel as if we&#8217;re owning something. But the fact of the matter is that we hardly own anything. These companies have us wrapped around their fingers because they provide us with entertainment and we&#8217;ll do just about anything for it.</p>

		<p>Audrey Watters discusses the “Domain of One’s Own” and the ideas behind the phrase. One of the key points she makes, which I find somewhat daunting, is that “the ‘domain of one’s own’ isn’t owned; its leased…we own little to nothing” (Watters). People think by paying, that they own that content, but that is rarely the case. I like the words that Sarah used, that companies are tricking us into thinking that we own something. In the past with media like books and dvds, the idea of ownership was much more concrete; you bought a book and now you own it forever. However, now lots of media is subscription based, so although you are consuming content, once you unsubscribe, that consumption stops. Pay once for a book, and you can read it as much as you want. Many have required this false sense of the “ownership society” that President Bush once promised. Watters makes a point that many Americans are ignorant of how the domain of one’s own operates. According to Bush, “Americans would and could and should own their own house…they would and could and should have to pay for their own education” (Watters). This idea never existed for everyone, and according to Watters, we now live in this “post-ownership society,” where virtually nothing is owned. How are we going to reverse this? Unfortunately, we may not. However, Watters suggests that it can be “resist[ed] through education…we can challenge how the Web and Internet work…but we can do so only if we understand what’s at stake” (Watters). Schools and professionals should begin to advise others on what their domain includes and how to properly manage it. I find this similar to digital identity in that many problems arise due to the naivety of some. We will only be able to live in such a digital society if we know how to manipulate it and understand it.</p>
