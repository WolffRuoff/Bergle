						<h1 class="entry-title">
				<a href="http://sedehidiversityproject.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2013/09/02/a-voice-from-last-years-sdp-vernon-jordan-iii-reflects-on-his-stake-in-the-conversation/" rel="bookmark">A voice from last year&#8217;s SDP: Vernon Jordan III reflects on his stake in the conversation</a>
			</h1>
								</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p><strong><em>EDITOR&#8217;S NOTE</em>: Vernon Jordan III is a member of the 2013 SDP ensemble. This post was composed in late August &#8217;13 just after the Sedehi Diversity Project&#8217;s encore performance for the Muhlenberg community. It&#8217;s being published here for the first time.</strong></p>
<p>It&#8217;s been a week since I took the stage to enlighten, expose, and educate the Muhlenberg College community around issues of diversity &#8211; and I simply can&#8217;t believe it&#8217;s over. This summer, because of the Diversity Project, among other things, turned out to be very powerful &#8211; dare I say, personal?</p>
<p>At the last talk-back with audience members and the ensemble, someone asked: &#8220;(I.)Why did you get involved with this project? (II.)And what do you want us as an audience to take away?&#8221; My answer lies here.</p>
<p>I. I initially got involved with the project (aka auditioning) because, well, how could I not? Last year, when the uprising around these issues sparked up around MLK day I became very involved &#8211; quickly and not so quietly. For me, this was personal; and at the same time I was learning I wasn&#8217;t the only one who faced discrimination, loneliness and a sense of unease on this campus. I knew that in addition to my role in multicultural organizations on campus, the wonderful experience I had in Memphis last semester, and the classes I took, that this would be a grand opportunity to do some further learning, and further growing.  This project was powerful to me, the project spoke to me &#8211; and I could not deny that. I felt like it was linked to my path here at Muhlenberg, and as I discovered during performances of the piece, it&#8217;s linked and connected to a large number of our paths here on Muhlenberg&#8217;s campus. The diversity project was an avenue for me to be vocal &#8211; and a lot more truthfully than in normal circumstances. Lastly, the project is itself an intersection of Art and Activism &#8211; something that is very real and very true. So to be involved with a project that straddles on both of those lines &#8211; again, how could I not?</p>
<p>II.  I wanted the audience to leave the performances of the piece challenged. And not only challenged, but motivated, fired-up, shocked, and ready to do something about the collision of ideas, conflict, identities and, honestly, emotion that actually happens on this campus as a result of its current systematic state. Ultimately, how I felt throughout the process (like I had to rethink a couple of things, maybe read a dictionary or two, like I needed to be open to opinions and beliefs I wasn&#8217;t used to, like I needed to spread this information around as opposed to letting it sit in my experience and in my brain, like I needed to continue to work and push for social justice), I wanted that replicated in the audience. If I, as an ensemble member, learned so much throughout the process, then you guys as audience members &#8211; the folks we intended on teaching and showing &#8211; should DEFINITELY had walked away with something new, something scary, something challenging to your world (whatever personal lens you live inside).</p>
<p>***The diversity project was a wonderful time for me, especially considering I&#8217;m the youngest member of this year&#8217;s ensemble and a lot newer to the Muhlenberg community than some of my fellow ensemble members. I will say this: the avenues in which you, as a student, can learn and be involved are numerous &#8211; so if you&#8217;re a student here reading this, freshman or not, GET INVOLVED! Talk to someone. Open your eyes (don&#8217;t be afraid to be wrong). Recognize your Power &#8211; and dammit use it! If you&#8217;re attending this institution, which prides itself in students being<em> &#8220;independent critical thinkers who are knowledgeable about the achievements and traditions of diverse civilizations and cultures, able to express ideas with clarity and grace, committed to life-long learning&#8221; </em> then we, you, and myself &#8211; the student body! &#8211; best start acting like it. Our work is not done.</p>

				<a href="http://sedehidiversityproject.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2013/08/23/why-i-chose-to-be-a-part-of-sdp/" rel="bookmark">Why I chose to be a part of SDP</a>
			</h1>
								</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p>After today&#8217;s showing for the RAs and SAs, a gentlemen asked us why each of us members of the cast wanted to be a part of SDP and what we hope the incoming freshmen, and anyone else who watches us, learn from the performance.  And here is my response.</p>
<p>When I first saw information about auditions for this year&#8217;s Diversity Project, I wasn&#8217;t completely sure if I wanted to go for it or not.  It wasn&#8217;t until later on that I realized how much I needed to audition and be a part of this process.  Last semester I was taking three classes in which they all discussed racial issues in education and the dance community.  These conversations mainly focused on racial issues between black and white.  As a half white, half Asian woman I became so frustrated by this.  In all my educational years, there is so little mentioned about Asian Americans in a historical and current context, so why should I be so shocked that this was still the case in college.  I realized in that moment that by not at least auditioning for SDP I was risking the possibility of an Asian American&#8217;s voice not being heard.  I became sick and tired of being left out, of being cast aside as not important, not a concern when that is the precise reason why it IS a concern.  By being part of this process I can assure that the issues faced by Asian Americans on campus are brought to light and included.  Now that we&#8217;re at the very end of this project, I can gladly say that I think I did my part and I&#8217;m very happy that this particular concern was addressed.</p>
<p>As for what I hope students, faculty, administration, etc. learn from this year&#8217;s performance.  Well I hope they understand that there is, in fact, a problem concerning diversity on our campus and that they see it as more than just a passing sentiment.  I want them to care and be concerned with the issue just as much as I am.  I don&#8217;t want people to be afraid to feel uncomfortable but understand that feeling uncomfortable is okay.  These discussions are hard especially the realizations and truths that come to light as a part of it.  But I think there is a brighter light at the end of the tunnel and what I hope is that people understand how much of a disservice is being done to the Muhlenberg Community by lacking diversity and lacking these sorts of discussions.  I want people to care; I want people to want to care.  I also want our audiences to realize that their general conceptions and supposed knowledge of the issues and concerns are not enough.  Things need to change on this campus, and yes the state of America in relation to this isn&#8217;t perfect either, but I think Muhlenberg may just be a bit behind as well.  It&#8217;s time for Muhlenberg to &#8220;wake up&#8221;.</p>

We ran out of time before all of us could answer, and so I am posting my response here:</p>
<p><strong>Why get involved with this?</strong></p>
<p>For those who don&#8217;t know me, I&#8217;m a white, straight, upper-middle class male. My only salient minority identity is the fact that I&#8217;m Jewish. So I have a enormous amount of privilege. My first year at Muhlenberg, I just sort of coasted by, absent in conversations on diversity, partially because I didn&#8217;t know they were there. But a lot of my friends were active in them. So I knew they existed, I just never thought that they had to do with me. I auditioned for the play going into sophomore year, and made it as an alternate that was never called upon.<br />
I wish I could pinpoint the moment where I realized how important this all was. I remember my mind exploding when I saw SDP as a first year, and I&#8217;d like to think that it had just been incubating since. But some point during sophomore year, I got involved. I started going to SQuAd meetings (Students for Queer Advocacy) and Muhlenberg&#8217;s Feminist Collective. I also interviewed and got into a group called Peer 2 Peer, which exists to facilitate conversations around diversity. I took a class on Gender and Sexuality in Judaism. I saw SDP my sophomore year. I saw the Vagina Monologues. I&#8217;ve had the fortune to be in a relationship with somebody who also cares about this stuff. I started my own blog (which I don&#8217;t think I can solicit here) so that I had a space to put all my ideas on these and other subjects. And somewhere inside this fire burned, this <em>need</em> to be active in these discussions.</p>
<p>I tried out again and made it. And it was one of the greatest experiences of my life.</p>
<p><strong>What do you hope people take away from it?</strong></p>
<p>As a white, straight, upper-middle class male, I came into these talks not knowing what to do with my privilege. The hope is that this project shows some ways to do that. But what I most want people to take away from this is that <strong><em>you</em> can be active in these discussions.</strong>  Yes, you reading this right now! All of you.You have power, the ability to change someone&#8217;s mind for the better. You won&#8217;t always be right, and you&#8217;ll make mistakes often. But that&#8217;s okay! Mistakes are fine as long as you learn from them. Go out there and engage with people! Challenge yourself! Fight for freedom for all people.</p>

						<h1 class="entry-title">
				<a href="http://sedehidiversityproject.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2013/08/23/slut-shaming/" rel="bookmark">Slut Shaming</a>
			</h1>
								</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p>Hi all! So we’ve started doing performances, and it’s been really exciting! Two performances down, eight to go! And I’m starting to get packed for my jetsetting off to India (right after our last performance.) This whole week is a little crazy.</p>
<p>But I want to go back to those first two performances. One of the things we mention in the play is slut shaming, and there’s been a lot of confusion over just what, exactly, slut shaming is. I figured this blog was the perfect place to put a slightly more comprehensive definition.</p>
<p>Slut shaming is a method by which society discourages people from having sex. Slut shaming is generally a tactic aimed at women, as women are typically held to a more rigid standard of sexual conduct, but a person of any gender can be slut shamed. It is when other community members make comments about the person’s behavior or presentation that admonish them for being more sexual than society deems acceptable.</p>
<p>A person does not actually have to be engaging in any amount of sexual encounters to be called a slut. It is not about behavior, it is about perceived behavior.</p>
<p>The reason slut shaming is bad is that it shapes our perception of rape. If a person experiences slut shaming, it can damage their self perception in the future. But it also devalues their worth as a person. If other people view them as a slut, they are viewing that person as “easy.” That person then becomes the target for rape or sexual harassment because they lack the agency to say no. If they are a slut they are expected to comply with an sexual advance at any time, regardless of if that advance is wanted and reciprocated, or not. Rape allegations from women who are deemed sluts by broader society are taken less seriously, although the event may be just as traumatic for the survivor.</p>
<p>Slut shaming goes hand-in-hand with victim blaming as part of the rape culture that we live in. But you’ll hear more about victim blaming in the show! And maybe I’ll come back and write more about that soon.</p>
<p>Hopefully this clears things up for those of you who were unsure what slut shaming was. The reference may make more sense now. Enjoy the show!</p>
