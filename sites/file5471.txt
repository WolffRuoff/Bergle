			<p>Hallelujah! The script is finished!</p>
<p>Rehearsal started at 6pm Friday August 9th and we did not rest until it was finished&#8230;..at 8am Saturday August 10th.  Is this considered dedication or insanity?</p>
<p>Although it was a somewhat grueling process, we were fortunate to not come across any truly difficult arguments amongst us.  That being said I think I can speak for the entire cast when I say that cutting some of the various sections and conversations was somewhat heart-breaking and disappointing.  Overall, however, I think we were successful and I&#8217;m looking forward to getting started memorizing our lines and really getting into rehearsing and preparing for the performances!</p>

						<h1 class="entry-title">
				<a href="http://sedehidiversityproject.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2013/08/08/define-diversity-go-okay-wait-this-is-hard/" rel="bookmark">Define &#8220;diversity&#8221; &#8230; GO! (Okay wait, this is hard&#8230;)</a>
			</h1>
								</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p>For the past 7 years, as part of my work advising the <strong>Sedehi Diversity Project</strong>, I’ve been privileged to partake in some amazing conversations with Muhlenberg community members about what diversity means. Those conversations have taught me a lot, especially about the inaccurate assumptions people make about one another.</p>
<p>One of the biggest inaccurate assumptions I’ve encountered is that we all mean the same thing when we use the word “diversity.” Hoo boy, is this ever not the case! I’ve learned that there are a lot of different definitions of diversity floating around out there. A. Lot.</p>
<p>One of the most common ones is that diversity refers to the multiple ways people can be different from one another. And I guess that’s not really inaccurate, at least not when you think about the word divorced from history.</p>
<p>The problem is though, when you do that – when you think about diversity as disconnected from a history in which some people have been systematically oppressed – you end up in conversations about “diversity of shoe-size” and “diversity of interests.” And is that really what we want to be talking about? Sound boring and pointless? You&#8217;re feelin&#8217; me here, aren&#8217;t you?</p>
<p>I&#8217;m thinking about this because today someone asked me what my definition of diversity was. And because it surprised them – and because it surprised <em>me</em> that it surprised them – I figured I’d get all bloggy and share it here.</p>
<p>To me, diversity refers to <strong>the work that communities do</strong> to expose and dismantle the systems of oppression that inhibit the thriving presence and influence of people who have been historically underrepresented, or people who have marginalized identities. In that way, I guess diversity is more ‘verbish’ than ‘nounish’ to me. It&#8217;s work. It&#8217;s work that we all have the responsibility to do.</p>
<p>That’s my definition. What’s yours?</p>

	<article id="post-29" class="post-29 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-8749 category-about-the-project tag-freshman-experience tag-sdp2011">
				<header class="entry-header">
			
						<h1 class="entry-title">
				<a href="http://sedehidiversityproject.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2013/08/07/seeing-the-project-a-freshman-experience/" rel="bookmark">Seeing the Project (A Freshman Experience)</a>
			</h1>
								</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p>When I first came to Muhlenberg as a freshman I was hesitant about the college for a lot of reasons. Luckily, orientation demonstrated that I had come to a really great place. Especially seeing the Sedehi Diversity Project for the first time.</p>
<p>Myself, along with one hundred or so freshmen filed into the performance space in our orientation groups, and I was completely unsure of what to expect. Orientation puts you through the wringer &#8211; you&#8217;re busy constantly and completely over stimulated by all the new people you&#8217;re meeting and new experiences you&#8217;re having. That coupled by the hurricane and everyone was a little frazzled. Once we were all seated, the director of the project stood up and introduced the play &#8211; I don&#8217;t even remember what she said &#8211; and then it began.</p>
<p>Students just started talking. And saying things I&#8217;d never heard anyone say before.</p>
<p>The diversity project talks about a lot of topics. Generally, we cover discussions of race, class, gender, sexual orientation, religion, ability (in terms of physical disabilities, learning disabilities, and mental health), body image, and Muhlenberg’s relationship to Allentown.</p>
<p>I honestly did not know people were allowed to talk about these issues. I’d been taught all my life that it was rude to talk about race or class or religion or ability, and why bother talking about sexual orientation or gender – non-normativity in these areas doesn’t exist. And suddenly my peers were all around me being blatantly honest about how they felt.</p>
<p>I realized I fit somewhere. That things I’d been experiencing in my life were real. And that conversations about diversity aren’t rude, they are 100% necessary. All of the time. For every.</p>
<p>I cried during my first viewing of the Sedehi Diversity Project. Four separate times. I’d never thought about ways in which core aspects of my identity had an impact on positive and negative experiences of life. I’d always thought it was just my life and those experiences happened detached from any sense of identity. Just to me. But as it turns out, those positive and negative life experiences are attached to aspects of identity that inform how you interact with everything and everyone!</p>
<p>After the Diversity Project, orientation groups have lengthy discussions about what they’ve seen and their feelings regarding diversity. My orientation group, together, grappled with the loss of the naïve perspective our high schools had taught us. We were shocked by the play and shocked by each other. That day 4 of us came out to each other (be it in official discussion or in conversation amongst ourselves). I’d never come out to any one before and here I was coming out to whole groups of people! Because SDP told me I could!</p>
<p>I think the Sedehi Diversity Project, more than anything else, connects people. It facilitates conversations with people who are different. It facilitates conversation with people who are the same (but didn’t know they were the same at all).  And it starts the most important conversation – a conversation about power and access and how we all interact with each other and the world</p>
					</div><!-- .entry-content -->

	<article id="post-116" class="post-116 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-8749 category-about-the-project tag-about tag-ensemble tag-interviews tag-process tag-script tag-sdp tag-transcriptions">
				<header class="entry-header">
			
						<h1 class="entry-title">
				<a href="http://sedehidiversityproject.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2013/08/06/sdp-whats-going-onu/" rel="bookmark">SDP: What&#8217;s going on</a>
			</h1>
								</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p>Ben did a great job giving a brief overview of what we&#8217;ve been working on and what&#8217;s to come in terms of SDP.  I want to go a little bit further in this post and talk a bit about why the Project is structured the way it is.</p>
<p>The Sedehi Diversity Project is a student-created, student-led piece of documentary theatre.  What that essentially means is that in addition to performing the piece, Ensemble members are involved in every aspect of it&#8217;s creation, from interviews to script building to blocking.  And even though I&#8217;m the director per say, there&#8217;s a more collective view of the process in which we work together to envision and produce this piece of theatre.</p>
<p>Documentary theatre is also inherently unique.  All of the material from our script is born out of things that were said by the Muhlenberg community.  Every single line.  How we acquire these perspectives is by going out and doing interviews (over 50 this year) and then transcribing them from audio to text (if you thought that sounds time consuming, it is haha).  This is so important because this script is completely personalized to Muhlenberg campus; it&#8217;s not just a generalized play about diversity.  It reflects the attitudes and climate of the campus community in the year that the play was produced.  Having been in the project last year, I can already sense the ways in which the tenor of this year&#8217;s play will be different.</p>
<p>So we interview and transcribe, interview and transcribe.  We are now at the point where we are taking all of that material and putting it in conversation with one another.  This involves going through well over a hundred pages of potential script.  Our goal, once again, is to reflect the conversations that are occurring on campus, right now.  This is not just another play about getting along in spite of our differences.  This becomes a discussion of real discourse about diversity, both the good and the bad.  It is a call to action.</p>
<p>Once our script is produced, we will move from the rehearsal house and into the black box, where the piece is performed, to become acquainted with the space.  As it is not required to be involved in theatre to be in this play, some Ensemble members really explored that space before.  With the exception of myself and our wonderful Project Manager Will, the Ensemble will become embodiments of our community, delivering the perspectives of many different community members, many different bodies.</p>
<p>SDP is one of the most exciting projects that I&#8217;ve ever been involved in, and one of the most unique.  I don&#8217;t want to speak for the entire Ensemble (so you guys correct me if I&#8217;m wrong haha), but it has already affected all of us, allowed us to express our own perspectives while also challenging certain ideas that we held.  We hope it will do the same for all those who come to see it.</p>

	<article id="post-99" class="post-99 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-8749 category-about-the-project tag-rehearsalprocess tag-sdp2013 tag-transcriptions">
				<header class="entry-header">
			
						<h1 class="entry-title">
				<a href="http://sedehidiversityproject.blogs.muhlenberg.edu/2013/08/06/how-we-do-it/" rel="bookmark">How We Do It</a>
			</h1>
								</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p>Hello all! I thought that it would benefit y&#8217;all to know what exactly we&#8217;re up to over here at &#8216;Berg. So! Here&#8217;s how it works.</p>
<p><strong>Interviews</strong></p>
<p>We begin by interviewing members of the Muhlenberg community. This includes students, faculty members, working staff, administration, and anyone else we can find. All the ensemble members sit down and interview this person, which usually lasts anywhere from 20 minutes to an hour and 20 minutes.</p>
<p><strong>Transcriptions</strong></p>
<p>One of the ensemble members is assigned the interview to transcribe. This usually takes muuuuuch longer than the actual interview. I made the hilarious-in-retrospect-but-not-at-the-time mistake of transcribing my <em>entire</em> first interview, including stupid jokes, laughter, and a laundry buzzer.</p>
<p><strong>Review</strong></p>
<p>Every weeknight, we all gather in the Rehearsal House to go over whatever transcriptions we have. We project them onto a screen and go through them line by line, agreeing and disagreeing on what we think should go into the final script. This is where we currently are in the process.</p>
<p><strong>Scriptbuilding</strong></p>
<p>Soon, we&#8217;ll take all of the lines we like and hash them into a proto-script. Then we&#8217;ll cut it, cut it some more, continue to cut it, do some more removal of lines, cut it a final time, and have a script.</p>
<p><strong>Theater Things</strong></p>
<p>After that we&#8217;ll divide up lines, memorize them, block the show, and then it&#8217;s performance time!</p>
<p>So that&#8217;s how it goes. Can&#8217;t wait to see you all!</p>
